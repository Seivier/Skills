## Front Matter

Traditional software architecture, inspired by construction industry principles, treated architecture as something immutable, determined entirely before coding began and not changed during development. This preplanned architecture model aligned with waterfall processes where fixed requirements preceded architectural design. Martin Fowler's Foreword articulates how agile methods fundamentally challenged this paradigm. The rise of agile exposed that requirements constantly change as business necessity, and that preplanned architecture could not accommodate modern dynamism. Evolutionary architecture represents an alternative approach: architecture is a constant effort that works closely with programming, reacting both to changing requirements and to feedback from development. The core mechanism for evolutionary architecture is the use of fitness functions to monitor the architectural state and ensure guided evolution. The discipline involves different styles of evolvability and careful attention to long-lived data, a topic historically neglected. Conway's Law is identified as a central concern throughout evolutionary architecture practice.

Mark Richards' Foreword to the Second Edition explicitly rejects the building metaphor for software architecture. While comparing software structure to physical building structure was once useful for explanation, it breaks down fundamentally: software architecture must be malleable enough to change quickly, unlike physical buildings. Modern organizations face constant, rapid changes in business (mergers, acquisitions, new business lines, organizational restructuring, cost-cutting) and in technology (new frameworks, platforms, products). Software architectures must adapt at the same rapid pace to remain aligned with both business and technical environments. Practical example: during a major acquisition, software architectures supporting key applications must scale to new customer bases and adapt to accommodate new business functionality and practices. The second edition builds on first-edition concepts of guided and incremental change, introducing latest techniques in automated architectural governance and evolutionary data strategies.

The book is structured in three parts. Part I defines mechanisms and engineering practices teams can implement: techniques, tools, categories, and foundational information for understanding evolutionary architecture. Part II addresses structural design—design decisions that make evolution and governance easier, covering architecture styles and principles around coupling and reuse. Part III covers the holistic entanglement of evolutionary architecture with the broader software development process. All case studies are hosted within PenultimateWidgets, a surrogate company created to abstract real-world client examples while preserving concrete relevance; many second-edition case studies were contributed by colleagues from actual projects. Code examples and supplemental materials are available at evolutionaryarchitecture.com.

## Chapter 1. Evolving Software Architecture

The chapter begins by defining "bit rot" (software rot, code rot, software entropy) as the gradual deterioration of software quality over time—a phenomenon teams struggle with because modern systems contain thousands of interdependent parts subject to change, and the software development ecosystem itself is dynamic, constantly introducing new tools, frameworks, and best practices. The authors use a metaphor of a unicyclist constantly adjusting boxes while maintaining balance to illustrate how architects must both balance competing concerns and adapt to new equilibrium states as innovations disrupt the status quo.

A concrete example shows how containerization (Docker) was not a sudden invention but emerged organically from incremental ecosystem shifts: commercial operating systems became free (Linux), DevOps practices like Puppet and Chef made them operationally free, and once the ecosystem became free and standardized, portable formats (Docker) became inevitable. Similarly, microservices did not exist in the year 2000 because the supporting infrastructure (cheap compute, DevOps automation, continuous delivery practices) was unavailable—the ecosystem had to evolve first.

The authors partition software architecture into two aspects: the *domain (requirements)* and *architecture characteristics* (also called nonfunctional requirements, system quality attributes, cross-cutting requirements, or "-ilities"). Characteristics like scale and performance drive market success; others like modularity and evolvability contribute to long-term maintainability. The challenge is that architects historically viewed architecture as governance activity, preventing change—but the modern paradigm requires mechanisms that *enable* change while *protecting* critical architectural characteristics, analogous to how unit tests protect code quality.

The central definition of *evolutionary architecture* has three components:

1. **Guided Change**: Teams identify important characteristics and employ *fitness functions*—objective functions adapted from evolutionary computing that measure how well a design achieves the "set aims." In evolutionary algorithms, fitness functions determine if a variant has improved; in architecture, they evaluate whether changes degrade important characteristics like latency, cohesion, security posture, or modularity. Fitness functions encompass metrics, tests, and verification tools, making architectural decisions explicit and testable rather than implicit and subjective.

2. **Incremental Change**: Both during development and deployment. Developers evolve the codebase in small steps (easier to reason about, lower risk); services and features can coexist in parallel versions during deployment. The case study *PenultimateWidgets*—a large widget seller—illustrates this: when the star-rating service releases a new version supporting half-star ratings (instead of integer ratings), existing consumers need not upgrade immediately. Services can migrate at their own pace. PenultimateWidgets' DevOps practices include architectural monitoring that automatically "garbage collects" (disintegrates) services when no route has touched them in a given time window, enabling autonomous cleanup without manual intervention.

3. **Multiple Architectural Dimensions**: Beyond just technical architecture (frameworks, libraries, languages), architects must consider data (schemas, table layouts, optimization), security (policies, tools, deficiency detection), and operational/system concerns (infrastructure mapping, servers, cloud resources). Drawing an analogy to classical physics (where Newton's fixed-point view evolved toward Einstein's relativity—realizing phenomena interact), modern architecture must view systems as multidimensional wholes. A change in one dimension (e.g., database schema evolution driven by technical decisions) affects others (security surface, performance, compliance). Figure 1-2 illustrates a complete architectural scope: requirements plus dimensions (auditability, data, security, performance, legality, scalability, etc.), each guarded by fitness functions.

Architectural governance has historically been a restriction mechanism; evolutionary architecture transforms governance into *automated protection*. Adding *evolvability* as a meta-characteristic—an architectural wrapper—ensures that as the system evolves, other critical characteristics (scalability, security, modularity) do not degrade. The example of a degraded layered architecture shows the problem: architects design layers to isolate change, but developers later bypass layers for perceived performance gains, increasing coupling and undoing the original reasoning.

On long-term planning: The programming platform landscape constantly shifts. Java emerged as a C++ replacement to improve networking and memory management. Languages continuously evolve their APIs and new paradigms appear regularly (Figure 1-3). Since the ecosystem changes unpredictably and traditional architecture was defined as "the parts that are hard to change later," teams faced a self-fulfilling prophecy where change remained expensive. The pivot: if *ease of change* becomes a bedrock architectural principle, then change is no longer difficult. This requires building changeability *into* the architecture itself rather than treating change as exceptional.

On why "evolutionary" and not other terms: *Incremental*, *continual*, *agile*, *reactive*, or *emergent* all capture change-over-time but miss the *guided* aspect—the desired end-state and the mechanisms constraining direction. The term *adaptable* suggests patching and jury-rigged solutions accumulating accidental complexity; *evolutionary* implies genuine, sustainable change. A common misconception in agile is "no architecture"—the truth is *no useless* architecture. Emergence (starting with nothing) differs from architecture (providing scaffolding and structure). Most problems can be solved with multiple architectural styles, but some fit better, offering less resistance. The balance between necessary structure/governance and needless formality is key.

## Chapter 2. Fitness Functions

Fitness functions form the primary mechanism for implementing evolutionary architecture. Defined as "any mechanism that provides an objective integrity assessment of some architectural characteristic(s)," they differ fundamentally from domain testing: while teams have specialized testing libraries for business logic (unit, functional, acceptance tests), architects must compose fitness functions from multiple tools across the ecosystem—a direct consequence of architecture's multidimensional nature.

The chapter illustrates this with Figure 2-1, showing that monitors (performance, scalability) via DevOps tools, code metrics (design criteria verification), chaos engineering (fault injection and resiliency), architecture testing frameworks (structure validation), and security scanning all serve as fitness function mechanisms.

The practical example of component cycles demonstrates why fitness functions matter. Cyclic dependencies between components are antipatterns (they entangle reusability), yet modern IDE auto-import dialogs reflexively create them. ArchUnit, a JUnit-inspired testing tool, detects cycles using a syntax like `slices().matching("com.myapp.(*)..").should().beFreeOfCycles()`, allowing architects to embed this check in continuous integration. This exemplifies how fitness functions prevent architectural drift without manual oversight.

The chapter then categorizes fitness functions across six dimensions:

**Scope**: *Atomic* fitness functions test one architectural aspect in isolation (e.g., cycle detection), while *holistic* fitness functions exercise combinations across shared contexts. A holistic example: security requires low data staleness, scalability demands caching; together they conflict, making holistic testing essential to detect real-world interactions.

**Cadence**: *Triggered* fitness functions execute on events (unit test runs, deployments, QA exploration), providing sparse data. *Continual* fitness functions run constant verification via techniques like synthetic transactions—production-like requests that follow normal architecture interaction flows (tracked by correlation ID) but don't commit results, enabling real-world performance observation without operational side effects. *Temporal* fitness functions incorporate time, such as reminders to upgrade deprecated libraries, back-port compatibility checks for upcoming framework versions, or dependency management via tools like Dependabot tracking when security patches become critical.

**Result**: *Static* fitness functions have fixed pass/fail outcomes (binary, ranges, set membership)—e.g., cyclomatic complexity thresholds. *Dynamic* fitness functions shift criteria based on context, such as adjusting response-time tolerances as concurrent users rise; they remain objective but context-aware.

**Invocation**: *Automated* fitness functions run in CI/CD pipelines; *manual* fitness functions rely on human verification, necessary for legal/regulatory requirements or exploratory testing. Both are valid when engineering practices demand.

**Proactivity**: *Intentional* fitness functions are defined at project inception through formal governance. *Emergent* fitness functions arise when architects notice misbehaviors benefiting from governance, added aggressively as the system evolves.

**Coverage**: Fitness functions govern purely architectural principles (elasticity, scalability, performance, security infrastructure), not domain logic. The litmus test: if a concern (like verifying a change-of-address) requires domain knowledge, it belongs to traditional testing; if it describes abstract architectural capability independent of use case (a gaming or streaming site both need elasticity), it's architectural.

The case study "Triggered or Continuous?" examines orchestrated microservices where only the orchestrator should hold workflow state. Direct inter-service communication (bypassing orchestration) corrupts state. The *continuous* approach: services publish monitoring/queue messages about collaborations; the orchestrator validates immediately. Benefit: immediate violation detection (security/correctness). Cost: runtime overhead (monitors, message queues) affecting performance/scalability. The *triggered* approach: deployment pipeline periodically harvests log files to check communication legality (shown in "Communication Governance in Microservices"). Benefit: no runtime cost. Cost: time lag, unsuitable for critical governance like security.

The section "Who Writes Fitness Functions?" clarifies that architects define fitness functions as objective measures; both architects and developers maintain them, and collaboration is critical so developers understand governance as useful constraint, not burden. The tip: post fitness function results visibly so developers remember them in daily coding.

The chapter notes that domain testing has "turnkey" frameworks (JUnit, xUnit variants) because domains use particular technology stacks. Fitness functions lack this because their scope spans operational, structural, security, and performance concerns—often requiring custom glue code. However, most projects can compose them with effort.

Finally, "Outcomes Versus Implementations" emphasizes focusing on *what* to measure (the outcome) over *how* (the tool). Example: PenultimateWidgets created an enterprise spreadsheet of desirable characteristics (scalability, security, resiliency) and reformulated them as testable fitness functions wired into the deployment pipeline, converting occasional ad hoc checks into continuous governance.

The *system-wide fitness function* collects individual fitness functions corresponding to architectural dimensions, enabling architects to weigh trade-offs uniformly. Unlike multifunction optimization (which may be impossible simultaneously), fitness functions help architects understand forces objectively—e.g., performance vs. security (encryption cost)—without resolving the trade-off, but clarifying it. Without this guidance, evolutionary architecture becomes merely reactionary.

## Chapter 3. Engineering Incremental Change

The chapter opens by distinguishing Jez Humble and Dave Farley's Continuous Delivery framework (which provides the operational mechanism through automation and tools) from evolutionary architecture (which provides the structure for evolvable design). Evolutionary architecture is formally defined as an architecture supporting guided, incremental change across multiple dimensions—meaning small, stepwise modifications rather than monolithic shifts. Two aspects are emphasized: development (how developers build) and operational (how teams deploy).

The PenultimateWidgets case study illustrates operational incremental change through a microservices architecture implementing share nothing architecture—each service is operationally distinct to eliminate technical coupling and enable granular change. The narrative traces deploying a new star rating service supporting half-star ratings alongside the existing version. Rather than forcing immediate migration, the architects use a proxy endpoint routing mechanism allowing gradual service migration over time. Operations monitors service routing using architectural monitoring (tracking routes between services, not just service health) and automatically garbage-collects unused service versions when no other services call them within a threshold interval. This approach exemplifies the mechanical ability to evolve—services can coexist, callers migrate at their own pace, and infrastructure cleanup happens automatically.

Deployment pipelines emerge as the primary engineering practice enabling incremental change. Unlike continuous integration servers (which run a list of post-check-in tasks), deployment pipelines explicitly model stages—discrete verification phases of increasing sophistication. This separation supports the pipeline's core mandate: verify production readiness through multistage builds, testing at multiple levels, automated environment provisioning, and comprehensive verification. A deployment pipeline automatically builds the deployment environment (using containers like Docker or infrastructure-as-code tools like Puppet and Chef) declaratively, ensuring consistent replication and high confidence in deployment.

Fitness functions integrate naturally into deployment pipelines as discrete stages. The pipeline executes arbitrary verification criteria at defined moments, running every system change through both atomic fitness functions (component-level) and holistic fitness functions (integration-level). The pipeline may perform fan-out operations (running multiple stages in parallel to test different scenarios) followed by fan-in consolidation (merging parallel threads into a single deployment decision). Feature toggles address the business tension between continuous deployment and staged releases: a condition in code (often checking an environment variable) enables or disables features at runtime, allowing teams to deploy frequently to production while controlling feature visibility, separating operationalization from release and enabling QA-in-production testing via selective user routing.

The invoicing service case study demonstrates a six-stage production deployment pipeline addressing scalability, service integration, security, and auditability concerns. Stage 1 replicates CI behavior (unit/functional tests). Stage 2 containerizes and deploys to dynamically created test environments. Stage 3 executes atomic fitness functions including automated scalability tests and security penetration testing, plus a metrics tool flagging changed code in audit-sensitive packages. Stage 4 executes holistic fitness functions including contract tests protecting integration points and further scalability tests. Stages 5a (manual security review) and 5b (manual audits per regulatory mandate) illustrate integrating regulatory requirements as fitness function stages—allowing engineers, auditors, and architects to reason about governance uniformly and quantify bottlenecks (e.g., if auditing happens monthly but security weekly, auditing is the release cadence constraint). Stage 6 deploys to production only if upstream manual reviews pass. Weekly automated reporting tracks fitness function success/failure rates, enabling health and cadence monitoring.

The API consistency case study illustrates a multipart, architecture-aware fitness function spanning pipeline stages. Stage 1 (Design) defines API changes in openapi.yaml. Tools like Spectral and OpenAPI.Tools verify specification structure and correctness. Stage 2 (Setup/Development) publishes the new specification to a sandbox environment and runs unit/functional tests verifying consistent application behavior. Stage 3 implements consumer-driven contracts using Pact testing tool, ensuring integration architecture contracts remain valid. Consumer-driven contracts are atomic integration fitness functions where consuming services provide test suites to the provider, who commits to keeping those tests passing. This shifts responsibility from ad-hoc manual verification to an engineering safety net: the provider executes tests on behalf of all consumers alongside their own tests, enabling evolutionary change without contract breakage. The final deployment stage uses feature toggles for A/B testing before official release.

The chapter concludes by contrasting software engineering with traditional engineering disciplines. In structural engineering, design and manufacturing effort is split roughly equally because manufacturing is expensive and design flaws are unforgiving. Software differs fundamentally: compilation and deployment (manufacturing) are cheap and automatable, so virtually all engineering effort concentrates on design (coding and architecture). Software's moving parts number in thousands or millions and can be changed arbitrarily and remanufactured instantly. The true software engineering discipline therefore rests on two pillars: incremental change (small, stepwise design modifications) and automated verification. Because manufacturing is free but variability high, confidence in making changes through automated fitness functions—not predictive mathematics—represents sanity in software development.

## Chapter 4. Automating Architectural Governance

The chapter frames fitness functions as the evolution of engineering governance practices, drawing historical parallels to continuous integration (early 1990s XP movement) and DevOps automation. The core problem: architects traditionally lacked tools beyond manual code reviews, architecture review boards, and other inefficient processes—important violations fell through cracks. Fitness functions solve this by binding governance to CI/CD pipelines with objective measures and fast feedback.

Code-level fitness functions leverage metrics defined in structured design (Yourdon and Constantine, 1979):
- Afferent coupling (incoming connections) and efferent coupling (outgoing connections) measure density and interconnectedness; high coupling creates Big Ball of Mud architectures where changes ripple across many components.
- Tools like JDepend (Java), IntelliJ Dependency Structure Matrix, and SonarQube visualize coupling matrices per package/component.

Robert Martin's derived metrics (late 1990s) address internal balance:
- Abstractness (A) = abstract artifacts / (abstract + concrete artifacts); measures abstract vs. implementation ratio. Pure code (only main() and 10,000 lines) scores near zero; abstractions improve understandability.
- Instability (I) = efferent coupling / (efferent + afferent); measures volatility/brittleness. I near 1 is highly unstable (many outgoing connections, few incoming). I near 0 is either stable (mostly abstract) or rigid (mostly concrete).
- Normalized distance from main sequence: D = |A + I - 1|; ideal relationship between abstractness and instability. Components close to this line are balanced; upper-right corner enters "zone of uselessness" (too abstract); lower-left enters "zone of pain" (brittle, little abstraction). Tools like NDepend visualize this graphically, revealing problematic packages that have drifted.

Cyclomatic complexity (CC, McCabe 1976): graph-theoretic measure of function complexity via decision points. Formula: CC = E - N + 2 (nodes and edges in control flow). CC=1 for no decisions; +1 per conditional. Industry thresholds suggest CC < 10 acceptable, but authors prefer < 5 for well-factored code. Overcomplex code harms modularity, testability, deployability. "Herding" instead of hard thresholds: cascade warnings over time (e.g., 15 → 10) to let teams address technical debt gradually.

Directionality of imports: JDepend API enables unit tests (Example 4-1) defining package dependencies; tests catch violations before commit (e.g., util should not import from persistence). This consolidates rules as executable artifacts, reducing bureaucratic enforcement.

Turnkey tools automate governance without custom code:
- Black Duck: monitors open source license changes during dependency updates, notifying lawyers if a license changes between versions (PenultimateWidgets case study: fitness function tracked license file hashes across versions).
- Pa11y: CLI scanner for web accessibility (A11y = a + 11 letters + y), enabling automated a11y governance.
- ArchUnit (Java): testing framework inspired by JUnit, designed for architectural rules rather than general code. Uses Hamcrest matchers for language-like assertions (Example 4-3 through 4-8). Supports: package dependencies ("no classes in package X should depend on Y"), class dependency rules (e.g., "Bar classes only accessed by Bar"), inheritance constraints (e.g., "Connection implementers end in 'Connection'"), annotation checks (e.g., "only @Transactional classes access EntityManager"), layer checks (e.g., "Controller never accessed, Service only by Controller, Persistence only by Service"). NetArchTest provides equivalent .NET capabilities. Principle: written architecture principles in wikis are ignored without enforcement; fitness functions make principles executable.
- Linters (ESLint, pyLint, cppLint, Staticcheck for Go, sql-lint) provide platform-specific governance hooks for non-Java/C# ecosystems; less powerful than ArchUnit but still enforce structural checks.

Integration and deployment architecture:
- Communication governance in microservices (Example 4-10): architectures often mandate single orchestrators with domain services forbidden from direct communication. Fitness function parses standardized log messages (service name, correlation ID, method, etc.) across 24-hour windows; scripting code (Ruby/Python) iterates logs, checks all calls target orchestrator. Trade-off: reactive fitness functions (log-based) avoid runtime overhead but catch violations a day later; proactive (real-time monitors) catch violations instantly but add runtime cost. Choice depends on governance criticality (security = proactive; structural = reactive).
- Message reliability (correlation IDs): grading workflow example (Figure 4-18 through 4-21) demonstrates choosing between container-level message counting (simple, atomic, service-level assurance) vs. end-to-end correlation ID tracking (holistic, requires state coordination). Real project exposed unreliable messages via fitness function charts, triggering forensic analysis and keeping guardrails in place.

Case studies demonstrate fitness functions beyond code:
- PenultimateWidgets availability: stress-tested legacy system with fitness function to prove it handled integration load, avoiding wasteful rewrite.
- Canary releases: auto-scaling tested via fitness function controlling feature flag to gradually release to small user subsets, monitoring performance increases as confidence grows.
- What to port decision: logging menu features in legacy Swing app revealed usage patterns (invoicing/lookup most used), prioritizing web port features and eliminating unused subsystems.

DevOps and chaos engineering:
Netflix Chaos Monkey and Simian Army exemplify holistic, continual operational fitness functions. Members: Chaos Monkey (random latency/reliability failures), Chaos Gorilla (entire datacenter outage), Chaos Kong (availability zone loss), Doctor Monkey (health monitoring), Latency Monkey, Janitor Monkey (orphaned services), Conformity Monkey (REST endpoint governance), Security Monkey. Continual execution (not scheduled tests) forced architectural resilience and enabled Netflix to survive Amazon outages. Evolved into Swabbie and other cloud-native fitness functions.

Enterprise architecture:
Fitness functions apply across the ecosystem. Enterprise architects define platforms hiding implementation via managed contracts (Figures 4-22 through 4-23); fitness functions govern platform capabilities, structure, APIs rather than technology choices. This prevents "Frozen Caveman Antipattern" (architects stuck on pet concerns). Evolutionary architects harvest fitness functions from projects, make them generic, build reusable platforms with clear quantum boundaries and contracts.

GitHub case study: architectural restructuring while deploying 60 times/day. Scientist framework (open source Ruby tool, Example 4-11, 4-12) implements fidelity fitness functions: runs old (control, use block) and new (candidate, try block) code paths in parallel, always returning control result but logging candidate differences. Decides whether to run experiment (1% users tried new merge in case study), randomizes execution order (mask accidental dependencies), measures durations (A/B performance testing), compares results, swallows but logs exceptions, publishes data. After 4 days and 24 hours of no mismatches, deprecated old code. Exemplifies continuous, holistic fitness functions enabling confidence in critical refactors.

Fidelity fitness functions generally preserve behavior during replacements via side-by-side old/new comparison; proxy allows controlled switch between implementations as discrete functionality is ported.

Implementation philosophy:
- Fitness functions are checklists, not sticks: like surgical and aviation checklists, they prevent skipping steps under schedule pressure. Architects must collaborate with developers; without enforcement, design principles fade in crisis.
- Fitness functions convert metrics/measurements into governance via objective measures and fast feedback loops; wiring into continuous verification is key.
- Most are simple: 10-20 lines of "glue" code in Python/Ruby combining existing tools (e.g., parsing logfiles, querying databases).

Documentation approaches vary:
- Fluent code itself (ArchUnit, Cucumber-style assertions) serves as documentation.
- Architectural Decision Records (ADRs) include governance sections.
- Behavior-driven development (Cucumber) maps native language scenarios to code (Examples 4-13, 4-14: Feature/Scenario describes intent, @Given/@When/@Then annotations map to methods).
- Literate programming tools: Jupyter notebooks (data science) combined with structural analyzers (jQAssistant) and graph databases (Neo4j) allow architects to document rules and execute queries (Example: Cypher query checking all classes in non-model packages implement @Entity annotation).

Metaphor: fitness functions are guardrails on roads of different materials (asphalt, gravel, cobblestones); regardless of road type or vehicle, guardrails keep travelers on path. Fitness functions are architecture characteristics guardrails preventing system rot and supporting evolution over time.

## Chapter 5. Evolutionary Architecture Topologies

Connascence: Foundation for Coupling Analysis
Connascence, a concept introduced by Meilir Page-Jones in 1996, provides a refined vocabulary for discussing coupling. Two components are connascent if a change in one requires modification of the other to maintain system correctness. Static connascence includes source-code-level coupling: Connascence of Name (CoN) where components agree on entity names (most desirable, refactoring-friendly); Connascence of Type (CoT) where components agree on type constraints; Connascence of Meaning (CoM) or Convention (CoC) where components agree on value meanings (e.g., magic numbers instead of named constants); Connascence of Position (CoP) where parameter order must align; and Connascence of Algorithm (CoA) where algorithms must match identically across components.

Dynamic connascence concerns runtime behavior: Connascence of Execution (CoE) where operation order matters; Connascence of Timing (CoT) where execution timing impacts outcomes (race conditions); Connascence of Values (CoV) where multiple values must change atomically (problematic in distributed systems with separate databases); and Connascence of Identity (CoI) where independent components must reference the same entity (e.g., distributed queues).

Three connascence properties guide architectural decisions: Strength measures refactoring ease (static preferable to dynamic; weak forms like CoN better than strong forms like CoA); Locality observes that high connascence is acceptable within the same module but damaging across module boundaries; and Degree measures impact scope. Jim Weirich formalized this as the Rule of Degree (convert strong forms to weaker forms) and Rule of Locality (use weaker forms as distance between elements increases).

Connascence and Bounded Context
Domain-Driven Design's bounded context concept aligns with connascence's locality principle. Both recognize that coupling spread across broad scopes creates brittleness—small changes cause unpredicted breakages elsewhere. Exposing a database schema as an integration point exemplifies this problem: when implementation details leak beyond bounded contexts, changes in one application may break others. The common architectural theme since at least 1993 is restricting implementation coupling to the tightest scope possible, whether expressed as bounded context or connascence locality.

Architectural Quanta and Granularity
An architectural quantum, borrowing terminology from physics (minimum physical entity in an interaction), is an independently deployable component with high functional cohesion including all structural elements required for proper system function. In monolithic architectures, the entire application is one quantum; in microservices, each service may form its own quantum.

Architecture quanta measure two coupling dimensions: Static coupling represents how static dependencies resolve through contracts (operating system, frameworks, transitive dependencies, operational requirements); Dynamic coupling represents runtime communication (synchronous or asynchronous). Static coupling describes how services are "wired" together; dynamic coupling describes how they "call" one another.

An architecture quantum formally requires: independent deployability (each quantum is a separate deployable unit, central to distributed architectures like microservices); high functional cohesion (proximity of related elements—classes, components, services—implementing single domain workflows); and synchronous dynamic coupling (specified in the formal definition, though asynchronous alternatives exist).

Independent deployability forces quanta boundaries to include databases and other operational dependencies. Shared databases prevent multiple quanta unless deployment occurs in lockstep. In monolithic architectures, deployable as single units, quantum measure is always one. Even distributed event-driven architectures can collapse to single quanta through central couplings: mediator-style event-driven architectures have single quanta due to the Request Orchestrator; broker-style EDAs using single relational databases for all services remain single quanta despite lack of mediators.

High functional cohesion distinguishes useful quanta from oversized monoliths that technically achieve independent deployability but encompass entire systems without cohesion. In microservices, each service models single domains achieving high cohesion; the quantum reflects the bounded context, not individual entities (e.g., CatalogCheckout as workflow boundary, not Customer as entity).

Static coupling analysis identifies actual coupling points. Microservices with separate databases and no interdependencies form multiple quanta. However, coupling through user interfaces reduces microservices architectures to single quanta: traditional UIs don't operate if backend portions are unavailable. Micro-frontend frameworks decouple this: UI elements emitted from services themselves, with loosely coupled event-based communication between components, allowing services plus corresponding UI components to form individual quanta.

Shared databases between systems create cross-system static coupling. Static quantum diagrams help understand "wired" architecture, determining which systems change impacts and decoupling opportunities. This technique increasingly applies to legacy architecture analysis.

Dynamic Quantum Coupling: Communication, Consistency, and Coordination
Dynamic coupling concerns synchronous runtime interactions. Three interlocking forces create multidimensional decision space: Communication (synchronous: requestor blocks until receiver responds; asynchronous: caller posts message, continues processing, receiver notifies via reply queue upon completion; asynchronous allows parallel behavior but lacks immediate feedback). Consistency describes transactional integrity: atomic all-or-nothing transactions versus eventual consistency degrees. Cross-service transactionality remains one of distributed architecture's most difficult problems, with general advice to avoid it.

Coordination concerns workflow complexity. Orchestration centralizes workflow control; choreography distributes coordination across services. Simple request-response patterns require no special coordination; complex workflows demand explicit coordination choices.

These factors interconnect: transactionality is easier with synchronous architectures and orchestration; higher scale emerges from eventually consistent, asynchronous, choreographed systems. A three-dimensional decision space (Figure 5-12) visualizes these forces, requiring architects to evaluate impact of changing any one factor on the others.

Contracts: Gluing Architecture Together
Contracts broadly define how disparate architecture parts connect. They encompass integration points (SOAP, REST, gRPC, XML-RPC), transitive framework/library dependencies, internal and external integration, caches, and any cross-part communication. Contract strength ranges from strict (requiring adherence to names, types, ordering, all details, leaving no ambiguity; Remote Method Invocation as strictest example) to loose (name/value pairs with no metadata or type information).

Strict contract examples include gRPC (RPC defaults to strict contracts matching method names, parameters, types). Strict contracts mimic internal method call semantics but create brittleness: frequently changing, multiply-used contracts cause rippling problems. JSON with schema information (Example 5-1 with $schema reference and required fields) exemplifies strict JSON contracts.

Looser contracts include REST (modeling resources rather than endpoints, making contracts less brittle—adding airplane engine details to seat query doesn't break existing queries) and GraphQL (aggregating read-only data from multiple services, reducing costly orchestration). Example 5-2 shows Customer Wishlist Profile containing only "name: String"; Example 5-3 shows fuller Customer Profile with address fields. Stamp Coupling antipattern assumes Wishlist might eventually need all fields, introducing unnecessary fragility: if Profile changes in ways Wishlist ignores, contract breaks.

Extreme loose contracts use pure name/value pairs (Example 5-4: {"name": "Mark", "status": "active", "joined": "2003"}) with no type information, schema, or metadata. Looseness enables extreme decoupling but trades contract certainty, verification, and increased application logic, often replaced by fitness functions. Keeping contracts at "need to know" level balances semantic coupling with necessary information without needless fragility.

Case Study: Microservices as Evolutionary Architecture
Microservices define physical bounded contexts between architectural elements, encapsulating parts that change. The bounded context serves as quantum boundary, including service code, databases, search engines, reporting tools—all contributing to delivered functionality. All architecture pieces operationalize together under DevOps practices (Figure 5-14).

Traditional role separation (architect/operations) requires coordination in evolutionary architecture; architecture remains abstract until operationalized. Quantum size determines lowest incremental change bound; small quanta enable faster change. Continuous Delivery combined with bounded context physical partitioning forms microservices' philosophical basis.

Layered architectures segregate by technical dimension (persistence, UI, business rules), lacking clear domain dimension. Domain concepts like Checkout smear across layers (Figure 5-15); achieving domain changes requires modifying each layer, yielding zero evolvability from domain perspective. Highly coupled architecture changes become difficult; yet common change units revolve around domain concepts. Role-silo organization matching layered architecture forces domain change coordination across many teams.

Microservices partition across domain lines, embedding technical architecture (Figure 5-16). Each service encapsulates technical architecture and dependent components (databases) into bounded context, creating highly decoupled architecture. Services own all bounded context parts, communicate via messaging (REST, message queues), and prevent implementation detail knowledge, preventing inappropriate coupling. Operational goal: replace service without disrupting others.

Seven microservices principles: Modeled around business domain (not technical architecture); quantum reflects bounded context as business workflow (e.g., CatalogCheckout) not single entity. Hide implementation details (database schemas not exposed across service boundaries). Culture of automation (Continuous Delivery, deployment pipelines, automated testing). Highly decentralized shared-nothing architecture (duplication preferable to coupling; if both CatalogCheckout and ShipToCustomer have Item concepts, each maintains own version, passing information without shared component coupling; shared components force coordinated changes). Deployed independently (each service deploys without affecting others, reflecting physical bounded context manifestation). Isolate failure (services handle error scenarios, employing Circuit Breaker patterns, bulkheads, Reactive Manifesto practices). Highly observable (hundreds/thousands of services require automated monitoring/logging as first-class concerns).

Microservices isolation via physical bounded context and domain understanding emphasis makes the service the architectural quantum, exemplifying evolutionary architecture. Service database evolution requires only that calling services receive identical integration point information, protected by fitness functions like consumer-driven contracts.

Incremental change: Each service's bounded context domain enables localized changes; reliance on Continuous Delivery automation supports safe, rapid change. Guided change with fitness functions: Well-defined service boundaries enable diverse testing levels; integration coordination via contracts requires sophisticated testing techniques alongside microservices development.

Reuse Patterns
Software reuse via frameworks and libraries has demonstrated benefits, but unbridled reuse creates problems. The axiom "Software reuse is more like an organ transplant than snapping together Lego blocks" (John D. Cook) reflects reuse difficulty. Making code reusable requires adding options and decision points; increased reusability hooks harm basic usability. Code ease-of-use is often inversely proportional to reusability.

Service-oriented architectures pursued ultimate canonicality: consolidating shared concepts (e.g., Customer service shared by Checkout and Shipping) created coupling. Microservices philosophy inverts this, adopting "prefer duplication to coupling": reuse implies coupling; microservices architectures are extremely decoupled. The goal isn't embracing duplication but isolating domain entities. Separate services having their own Item representation and communicating via information exchange (not component sharing) enables independent change. Duplication's localized damage outweighs architectural damage from excessive coupling. Nonetheless, some coupling benefits architecture: microservices commonly use service meshes coupling operational concerns (monitoring, logging) across services.

Sidecars and Service Mesh: Orthogonal Operational Coupling
Microservices design emphasizes decoupling, particularly data representation decoupling. Yet operational capabilities (monitoring, logging, authentication, authorization, circuit breakers) benefit from high coupling. Allowing each team independent implementation creates chaos; unified solutions require coordination mechanisms.

The Sidecar pattern, based on Alistair Cockburn's Hexagonal architecture (Figure 5-17), decouples domain logic from technical coupling. Hexagonal architecture places domain logic in center hexagon, surrounded by ports and adapters (Ports and Adapters pattern). While predating microservices, it treated database as adapter; DDD insight embeds data schemas and transactionality inside (like microservices).

Sidecar pattern leverages hexagonal architecture concept decoupling domain from infrastructure logic. Figure 5-18 illustrates services with operational concerns (bottom) separated from domain concerns (top). Consistent operational capability architectures move separable parts into sidecar components (metaphorically named for motorcycle sidecars), implemented either through shared team responsibility or centralized infrastructure groups. Assuming every service includes the sidecar forms consistent operational interface across services via service plane (Figure 5-19).

If architects safely assume every service includes sidecar component (governed by fitness functions), they form service mesh (Figure 5-20) where sidecar boxes interconnect, forming "mesh." This enables dashboards, operational characteristic control (scale), and diverse additional capabilities. Sidecar pattern allows enterprise architects reasonable polyglot environment restraint without single platform requirement.

Orthogonal reuse patterns address orthogonal coupling (two distinct purposes intersecting at right angles implying independence, e.g., monitoring independent from domain checkout but still required). Service mesh acts as distributed architecture Decorator pattern equivalent, "decorating" behavior independent of normal connectivity.

Data Mesh: Orthogonal Data Coupling
Data Mesh, derived by Zhamak Dehghani et al. from microservices decoupling, service meshes, and Sidecar patterns, applies these concepts to analytical data. It separates operational/analytical data's orthogonal coupling more complexly than operational coupling.

Data Mesh approach shares, accesses, and manages analytical data decentrally, satisfying reporting, ML training, and insight generation without central data warehouses or data lakes. Four principles: Domain ownership of data (domains originating or first-class consuming data own/share it peer-to-peer without intermediate warehouse transformations); Data as product (domains serve data as product, establishing organizational roles and success metrics for positive consumer experience, introducing data product quantum architectural concept); Self-serve data platform (domain teams build/maintain data products with declarative creation, discoverability, lineage, knowledge graphs); Computational federated governance (despite decentralized ownership, organization-wide governance—compliance, security, privacy, quality, interoperability—is met consistently through automated policies embedded as code in each data product via platform-supplied embedded sidecars).

Data product quantum (DPQ), core Data Mesh element, is an operationally independent but highly coupled adjunct to service quanta (Figure 5-21). Service Alpha contains behavioral/transactional operational data; domain includes DPQ containing code and data acting as interface to analytical/reporting system. DPQ acts as operationally independent but highly coupled behavior/data set.

DPQ types: Source-aligned (native) DPQ provides analytical data cooperatively with collaborating architecture quantum (microservice); Aggregate DPQ aggregates multiple inputs synchronously/asynchronously; Fit-for-purpose DPQ custom-serves particular requirements (analytics, BI, ML).

Multiple DPQs may exist per domain with different architecture characteristics (performance levels). Each contributing domain includes DPQ (Figure 5-22). Domain team owns DPQ, overlapping information stored in service database with asynchronous interactions with domain behavior. DPQ likely includes behavior and data for analytics/BI purposes.

Each DPQ acts as cooperative quantum: an operationally separate quantum communicating via asynchronous communication and eventual consistency featuring tight contract coupling with its cooperator and generally looser contract coupling to analytics quantum. While operationally independent, they represent data operational/analytical sides.

Analytics/BI responsibility forms own domain and quantum with static quantum coupling to individual DPQs needing information. This service makes synchronous or asynchronous DPQ calls depending on request type (e.g., SQL interface enabling synchronous queries; aggregations across DPQs requiring asynchronous approaches).

Data Mesh exemplifies innovative microservices/analytical data mashup, roadmapping orthogonal coupling management in distributed architectures. Sidecar and cooperative quantum concepts allow selective architecture overlay, preserving domain modeling (DDD) while allowing separate concerns well-governed access needs.

## Chapter 6. Evolutionary Data

Data must be treated as a first-class concern in evolutionary architecture, not an afterthought. Developers must apply the same engineering rigor to database schemas as to source code: they must be tested, versioned, and incremental. Database migration tools (delta scripts) enable this by automating small, idempotent schema changes applied through deployment pipelines. Migrations are treated as immutable ledgers similar to double-entry bookkeeping; once run, they are never modified. Instead, subsequent migrations undo unneeded changes. While some teams support undo capabilities (allowing forward/backward traversal through schema versions), most have moved away from them due to testing burden, redundancy of maintaining both directions, and the complexity of preserving data in reverse operations.

Shared Database Integration is a common but problematic pattern where multiple applications use a single relational database, fossilizing the schema across all coupled services. The Expand/Contract pattern (a subset of Parallel Change) solves this by introducing a transition state that maintains backward compatibility and gives other systems time to migrate. Three scenarios illustrate increasing complexity: (1) no integration points or legacy data—simple ALTER/DROP operations; (2) legacy data but no external systems—requires data migration functions like extractfirstname() and extractlastname(); (3) existing data AND integration points (most common)—requires database triggers (e.g., SynchronizeName) that bidirectionally synchronize the old name column with new firstname/lastname columns during the transition, with a final contraction phase that drops the legacy column after dependent systems migrate.

Inappropriate Data Entanglement stems from the maturity gap between data teams' tools and modern development practices. Data teams typically lack refactoring support, out-of-container testing, unit testing, linting, mocking, and dependency tracking—features standard in developer IDEs. Database vendors have cultivated loyalty among data practitioners, creating organizational silos resistant to external tools and Continuous Delivery practices.

Two-Phase Commit Transactions act as a strong nuclear force in architecture, binding architectural quanta together tightly. Unlike architectural coupling (easily visualized via dependency tools), transactional coupling is invisible yet deeply constrains evolution. Transactional boundaries often reflect how business processes are coupled; architects must consider this when defining service granularity. Attempting to build services smaller than the natural transactional context creates inappropriate coupling. When migrating monoliths to microservices, start with larger services first, respecting that each service should capture a useful bounded context—not an artificially small one.

Age and Quality of Data pose another evolution obstacle. When data teams refuse to refactor schemas, viewing schemas as immutable assets, they resort to workarounds like adding join tables instead of restructuring. This creates fossilized schemas layered with decades of accumulated abstraction hacks. Legacy data quality (inherited inconsistencies from prior software versions) further couples architecture to the past, forcing elaborate workarounds. Teams must have frank conversations: preserve data with true organizational value in mainstream flows, archive older data for reference, and actively refactor schemas to baseline quality.

The PenultimateWidgets routing case study demonstrates Expand/Contract at scale: the company implemented new routing (requiring more context: origin page, workflow state) alongside existing routing, with database triggers replicating changes between old and new route tables. Once dependent pages migrated, the old table and triggers were dropped.

In microservices (one database per bounded context), architects must address fitness function governance since distributed persistence sacrifices some relational guarantees. Referential integrity—enforced by primary key constraints in monoliths—requires event-driven synchronization in distributed systems: when a trader rejects a trade via Trader Blotter service, a message on a durable queue triggers updates/deletions in all interested services. Read/write separation is critical: teams define data ownership (who updates) versus read access (who consumes), often using in-process caching on startup with update frequency tuned per use case.

Stored procedures and triggers, powerful in monoliths, become liabilities in distributed architectures since data no longer resides in a single database. The Migrate Method from Database pattern (an Expand/Contract variant) extracts stored procedure logic into service code. During expand, a new service method acts as a pass-through to the legacy procedure; during contract, fitness functions verify all callers have migrated before dropping the procedure (a database Strangler Fig pattern). Alternatively, teams may choose broader data contexts to preserve triggers rather than refactoring them.

The final case study shows PenultimateWidgets breaking a monolithic relational database across microservices with heterogeneous persistence: catalog data to graph databases (better for categorization), analytics to specialized BI databases, operational data to key/value stores. This reflects the key benefit of distributed architectures—polyglot persistence enables problem-specific optimization, though it introduces trade-offs in complexity and consistency guarantees that architects must transparently surface to data teams.

## Chapter 7. Building Evolvable Architectures

The chapter opens by positioning evolutionary architecture as the intersection of mechanics (continuous delivery practices, testing, automation) and structure (architectural characteristics, coupling, cohesion). Five principles form the conceptual foundation: (1) Last Responsible Moment: delay architectural decisions until you have maximum information, avoiding overengineering while ensuring you don't miss critical constraints—the architect's job is trade-off analysis, which requires information; (2) Architect and Develop for Evolvability: treat evolvability as a first-class architectural concern, considering coupling, brittleness, and data/integration points with equal weight as code dependencies; (3) Postel's Law ("be conservative in what you send, liberal in what you accept"): minimize information in contracts, don't require consuming all available data, and use versioning only when contracts break; (4) Architect for Testability: systems hard to test are hard to maintain; testable architecture is more evolvable; single responsibility principle reduces testing burden; (5) Conway's Law: team structure shapes architecture.

The mechanics section outlines the operational process: Step 1 involves identifying which dimensions (technical architecture, data design, security, scalability, "-ilities") require protection; this requires cross-functional teams including business, operations, and security. Step 2 defines fitness functions (both automated and manual) for each dimension; fitness functions are collections of code metrics wired into deployment pipelines (e.g., preventing dependency cycles) or behavioral checks. Step 3 automates fitness functions within deployment pipelines, enabling incremental change and high-velocity feedback. Cycle time (a Continuous Delivery metric) is critical: longer cycle times reduce the velocity of architectural evolution. The identification of dimensions and fitness functions occurs at project inception but is also ongoing—unexpected failures reveal unaddressed dimensions that require retrofitted fitness functions to prevent future dysfunction.

For greenfield projects, evolvability is simpler to build in because deployment pipelines and fitness functions can exist from inception, preventing undesirable coupling from forming. Microservices architectures are highlighted as natural candidates due to low coupling and high incremental change potential.

Retrofitting existing architectures depends on three factors: component coupling, engineering practice maturity, and developer facility with fitness functions. Appropriate coupling and cohesion determine evolvability; the best technical architecture fails if the data schema is rigid. Functional cohesion of components determines ultimate granularity during migration—some domains are inherently more coupled (heavily transactional systems) and forcing extreme decoupling can be counterproductive. Continuous Delivery practices are nearly mandatory; teams in transition between old and new practices should include manual stages in pipelines to maintain awareness and provide feedback. The biggest impediment to evolutionary architecture is intractable operations—if developers cannot deploy changes easily, the feedback cycle breaks. The chapter distinguishes refactoring (restructuring code without changing behavior, per Martin Fowler) from restructuring (substantive changes to both structure and behavior). COTS (Commercial Off-The-Shelf) software poses severe evolutionary challenges: it resists incremental change, exhibits poor API coupling (the "Last 10% Trap"), offers limited fitness function visibility (forcing coarse-grained behavioral integration tests), and may embed the database as the integration point, complicating evolution on both sides.

Architecture migration typically follows a pattern from monolith to service-based architecture. Service granularity is critical—too coarse, and decomposition provides minimal value; too fine, and orchestration overhead and interdependency increase. Service boundaries can be identified via business functionality groups, transactional boundaries, or deployment goals (different release cadences for different domains). The chapter emphasizes gradual migration: first split into a few large portions, fix integration points, then repeat. UI separation is an early step, creating an anticorruption layer between frontend and backend. Service discovery mechanisms (simple proxy layers mapping to implementations) enable controlled migration. The migration process must balance the indirection required for discovery against the complexity it adds.

When decomposing shared modules, the Chidamber & Kemerer metrics suite—specifically LCOM (Lack of Cohesion in Methods)—helps determine if a module should be split or retained. LCOM scores measure failed opportunities to exploit coupling; high LCOM suggests a component should have been multiple components. Two strategies emerge: extract into a shared library (introducing coupling via JAR, DLL, etc.) or replicate the module across services (the microservices-preferred approach). Developers can also achieve sharing via messaging or service invocation in distributed environments.

The guidelines section addresses practical implementation challenges. Remove Needless Variability emphasizes immutable infrastructure (systems defined entirely programmatically, not hand-crafted "snowflake" servers) to control unknown factors and reduce vectors of change. Once bootstrapped, immutable systems accept no manual modifications. This applies to operations and development environments alike—capturing development environments as immutable infrastructure ensures all developers use identical tooling, eliminating "works on my machine" variability and enabling tool distribution across teams via pair rotation. A cautionary example (Knightmare/PowerPeg incident, 2012) illustrates the cost of snowflake servers: a company accidentally configured one server incorrectly, using the wrong feature toggle, leading to a $400 million loss in minutes—a stark reminder that unknown variability and failure to automate deployments are reckless.

Make Decisions Reversible: DevOps practices enable undoing failed decisions. Blue/green deployments maintain two identical ecosystems; if the green release fails, operations revert to blue without significant pain. Feature toggles allow canary releasing (deploying to a small user subset first) and rapid rollback if unexpected behavior occurs; critically, obsolete toggles must be removed aggressively. Service routing provides another canary-release method in microservices. Prefer Evolvable Over Predictable: unknown unknowns are the nemesis of software systems; projects cannot design for unknowns in advance. Dynamic equilibrium makes predictability useless; instead, architects should build evolvability into systems so that easy incorporation of changes eliminates the need for a crystal ball. Architecture is not solely a up-front activity—projects constantly change. Anticorruption layers insulate systems from unexpected change.

Build Anticorruption Layers: projects often couple to external libraries (message queues, search engines, etc.). The Abstraction Distraction antipattern describes over-coupling to external library abstractions, making upgrades or substitution painful. Domain-driven design offers the anticorruption layer pattern: a facade that shields domain logic from library implementation details. An extended example describes a Ruby on Rails car-auction application that deferred a message queue decision. Rather than prematurely adopting a "fancy" queue, architects chose BackgrounDRb (simple, database-backed) behind an API. One year later, a second asynchronous need appeared; the team added a second BackgrounDRb instance. Two years later, a third need revealed the approach's limits, and the team switched to Starling (a more traditional message queue). Because the original solution was isolated behind an interface, the transition took less than one week and didn't disrupt other developers. The chapter advises: think about the semantics (what you need) rather than the syntax (the API) when building anticorruption layers, but avoid preemptive overabstraction ("call a Factory to get a proxy to a remote interface to a Thing"). Modern IDEs enable Just In Time (JIT) anticorruption layers via extract-interface refactoring, applied when coupling becomes problematic.

Build Sacrificial Architectures: Fred Brooks (Mythical Man Month) advised "Plan to Throw One Away," recognizing that teams learn unknown unknowns only after building the first system. Martin Fowler defines sacrificial architecture as one designed to be discarded if the concept succeeds. Examples: eBay migrated from Perl (1995) to C++ (1997) to Java (2002); Twitter launched in Ruby on Rails for fast time-to-market, then rearchitected when scale caused crashes (the famous "Fail Whale" image). Starting with a sacrificial architecture enabled market entry; waiting for a final robust platform would have allowed competitors to dominate. Cloud environments make sacrificial architecture more attractive: initial versions require fewer resources, allowing early learning. However, teams must allocate time to build a robust architecture eventually. Fred Brooks also warned of second system syndrome: small, elegant, successful systems evolve into feature-laden monstrosities as expectations inflate and organizations resist decommissioning working code. Technical debt aggravates inappropriate coupling; the first step in restructuring should be removing historical design compromises.

Mitigate External Change: external dependencies (tools, frameworks, libraries) are inevitable and updated via the internet. The JavaScript leftpad incident (2016) exemplifies the hazard: a developer removed 250+ modules, including leftpad (11 lines of code to pad strings), breaking major JavaScript deployments. Dependencies provide benefits but impose costs (updates, management, constraint). Chris Ford's observation: "Transitive dependency management is our 'considered harmful' moment." Developers cannot build repeatable practices atop uncertainty; allowing third parties to change core dependencies violates evolutionary architecture's stability requirement. The recommended approach uses a pull model: maintain an internal version-control repository as a third-party component store, treating external updates as pull requests. Accept beneficial changes; reject destabilizing removals. Treat the third-party repository's own deployment pipeline like internal development: incorporate updates, build and smoke-test affected applications, release if successful. This blurs the line between in-house code and dependencies.

Updating Libraries Versus Frameworks: libraries and frameworks differ in coupling degree. Libraries are called by developer code (lower coupling); frameworks call developer code (higher coupling via subclassing). Developers should prefer libraries because they introduce less coupling and are easier to swap. Frameworks form the application's scaffolding, so changes impact all application code; teams must aggressively update frameworks (two major versions behind is painful). Libraries provide utilitarian functionality and can update via a pull model (when needed). An informal governance approach treats framework updates as push updates (apply immediately when stable) and library updates as pull updates (apply when new desired functionality appears).

Version Services Internally: integration architectures require service versioning as behavior evolves. Two patterns exist: Version Numbering (create a new endpoint with version number; old integration points call the legacy version) versus Internal Resolution (callers never change; logic inside the endpoint determines context and returns the correct version). Internal versioning reduces coupling to specific version numbers. In either case, support only two versions simultaneously to minimize testing burden.

The case study of PenultimateWidgets' star rating service illustrates evolution in practice. The original service (Figure 7-10) has a layered architecture (persistence, business rules, UI) backed by a relational database with an integer rating column. When the team decided to support half-star ratings, they added a new database column (Figure 7-11) and a proxy component at the service boundary to resolve differences between whole-star and half-star request/response formats via internal resolution routing. This allowed callers to request either format without understanding version numbers. Once all dependent services evolved to half-star ratings, the old code path and proxy were removed (Figure 7-12), leaving a simplified service. This additive approach to database schema changes differs from destructive ones; see Chapter 6 for evolutionary data design details.

Fitness Function-Driven Architecture applies test-driven development (TDD) principles to architecture: define fitness functions for critical capabilities before design, ensuring those capabilities remain top-of-mind. The LMAX architecture exemplifies this. Regulatory changes enabled online market participation, requiring millions of transactions per second. Starting with a fitness function measuring transaction speed, they experimented with thread-based designs (failed), actor models (failed), then discovered context switches were the bottleneck. They designed input/output disruptors using a single thread and ring buffers, achieving six million transactions per second on one thread. This process popularized the term mechanical sympathy (understanding layers underneath abstractions to optimize performance—how much time each part of a request/response takes, from network to computation). Once LMAX achieved their initial goal, fitness functions remained in place, governing subsequent evolution and redirecting the team when approaches conflicted with those objectives. Fitness Function-Driven Architecture ensures changes never compromise success criteria—similar to TDD in code.

## Chapter 8. Evolutionary Architecture Pitfalls and Antipatterns

The chapter opens by distinguishing pitfalls (superficially good ideas that immediately prove harmful) from antipatterns (practices that look good initially but only reveal themselves as mistakes in hindsight). Both restrict evolutionary capacity through inappropriate coupling.

In Technical Architecture, the Last 10% Trap manifests when tools promise 80% of functionality easily via drag-and-drop but fail catastrophically on the remaining 10% that wasn't built into the tool. Neal Ford observed this repeatedly with 4GLs like Microsoft Access; developers attempt hacks (scripting in static areas, method chaining) to reach 90%, but the architecture cannot solve the problem completely. Modern low-code/no-code environments exhibit identical patterns. The advice: evaluate hardest problems first, not easiest ones, to identify tool limits early and build alternatives.

PenultimateWidgets' reuse case study illustrates how a well-intentioned shared grid component became a bottleneck. The component team, initially heroes, became swamped maintaining a coupling point that the host organization depended on while underlying code stagnated against modern web standards. The architecture achieved reuse but violated dynamic equilibrium—the innovation pace of consuming teams exceeded the component team's maintenance capacity. Resolution required decoupling: allowing teams to fork the component, opt out entirely, or own shared code themselves. Key lesson: continually evaluate whether architectural assets still deliver value; break coupling when it impedes evolution or key "-ilities."

Vendor King antipattern describes architectures entirely centered on ERP or similar encapsulating tools. While appropriate for some scenarios, ambitious implementations create pathological coupling. Customization attempts via plug-ins fail because the tool cannot be sufficiently extended—a manifestation of the Last 10% Trap. The organization's business processes then bend to fit the vendor's constraints. The underlying Let's Stop Working and Call It a Success principle describes how multimillion-dollar ERP investments fail silently; neither vendor nor organization admits failure, calling partial implementations "success." Solution: treat all software as an integration point, using anticorruption layers to insulate architectural decisions from vendor changes.

Leaky abstractions emerge from the tower of software abstractions (OS, frameworks, dependencies). As Joel Spolsky observed, all nontrivial abstractions leak—no abstraction is perfect, or it would be the real thing. The problem worsens with tech stack complexity: 2005 stacks (simple OS + database + application framework layer) have exploded into modern architectures with dozens of specialized components. Primordial abstraction ooze describes how a fault at a low level (unexpected database call side effect) propagates upward through many layers, metastasizing and manifesting as a cryptic error deep in the UI. Debugging becomes forensically complex. Fitness functions at key integration points protect fragile joins by defining invariants that run in the deployment pipeline, preventing abstractions from leaking in undesirable ways.

Resume-Driven Development pitfall occurs when architects choose technologies for CV appeal rather than problem fit. The corrective advice: understand the problem domain first, then select the architecture that delivers desired capabilities with fewest damaging constraints.

In Incremental Change, the Inappropriate Governance antipattern reflects outdated economics. When operating systems, databases, and infrastructure were expensive commercial products, shared-resource architecture made sense (SOA flourished here). Governance models maximized resource sharing for cost savings. However, DevOps and containerization eliminated this constraint—developers now provision resources for near-zero cost. Homogenizing on a single technology stack became inappropriate; it creates inadvertent overcomplication. Monolithic architectures suffer worst: a database choice must satisfy all projects' most complex case, forcing simpler projects into industrial-strength systems. Microservices solve this by decoupling services technically and via data architecture, allowing teams to right-size technology complexity. Chad Fowler at Wunderlist insisted on different technology stacks across services to force decoupling and prevent accidental reuse—his philosophy: accidental coupling is a bigger risk than developer portability. Practical governance: the "just enough" model picks three standard stacks (simple, intermediate, complex) and allows requirements to drive stack selection.

PenultimateWidgets' governance evolution illustrates this: years of Java + Oracle standardization imposed complexity on small services. Three-tier solution emerged: Ruby on Rails + MySQL for simple projects; GoLang + Cassandra/MongoDB/MySQL for medium-complexity data requirements; Java + Oracle for large projects with variable architectural concerns.

Lack of Speed to Release pitfall decouples evolutionary architecture from engineering practice. Continuous Delivery practices address release bottlenecks. Two key metrics: lead time (idea to working software, includes subjective activities) and cycle time (feature start to production deployment). Cycle time measures engineering efficiency; the authors derive velocity ∝ cycle time, meaning evolution speed is bounded by cycle time. Faster releases enable faster architecture evolution. Fitness functions can monitor cycle time itself; a deployment pipeline achieving three-hour cycle time can set a fitness function threshold at four hours to raise alarms on degradation, creating decision points to weigh cycle time against added verification.

In Business Concerns, Product Customization pitfall describes sales pressure for infinite customization: unique builds per customer, permanent feature toggles (creating permutations), or product-driven UI customization. Each increases testing burden and required fitness functions. Organizations should realistically assess costs.

Reporting Atop the System of Record antipattern shows how reporting creates inadvertent coupling in monolithic architectures. A single schema serves neither order-entry nor reporting optimally. Layered architecture intends isolation, yet reporting bypasses layers and couples directly to database schemas, preventing schema evolution. Microservices solve via event streaming: services populate their own domain databases (eventual consistency); reporting services consume event streams into denormalized, reporting-optimized databases, allowing different abstractions for transactional and analytical concerns.

Excessively Long Planning Horizons pitfall describes how long budget cycles force early, irreversible decisions with minimal information. Sunk Cost Fallacy and Irrational Artifact Attachment (the more time invested, the harder to abandon decisions) follow. Remedy: break programs into smaller, early deliverables to test feasibility; avoid technologies requiring massive up-front investment before validating problem fit; keep options open.

## Chapter 9. Putting Evolutionary Architecture into Practice

Organizational Factors and Team Structure: The chapter opens by emphasizing that Conway's Law—organizations produce systems reflecting their communication structures—necessitates deliberate alignment between team topology and architectural goals. Technical function silos (frontend, backend, database teams) create coordination overhead when paired with distributed architectures like microservices; changing a concept like `CatalogCheckout` requires all three teams to coordinate, slowing development. The Inverse Conway Maneuver counters this by structuring teams around service boundaries rather than technical capabilities.

Cross-functional teams represent the ideal organizational pattern. Each team should encompass roles typically separated in silos: Architecture, Business Analysts, Data (DBAs, data analysts), Developers, Designers, Operations, Product Managers, and Testing. This structure eliminates operational friction—the team possesses all skills needed to design, implement, and deploy their service. The chapter cites Amazon's "two-pizza team" philosophy: no team larger than can be fed with two pizzas, motivated by reducing communication paths. Small teams also leverage human social dynamics; if a developer's code causes a production incident affecting their cross-functional colleague, they feel the impact directly, incentivizing quality.

Organizing teams around business capabilities rather than job functions ensures the common unit of change is handled within a single team. The authors reference Manuel Pais and Matthew Skelton's Team Topologies model, which identifies four team patterns: stream-aligned teams (aligned to a business flow), enabling teams (helping overcome obstacles), complicated subsystem teams (requiring specialized expertise), and platform teams (providing infrastructure to accelerate delivery). Crucially, team design must account for cognitive load—a team maintaining five concurrent payment schemes may exceed their cognitive capacity despite being aligned to one business domain, necessitating further subdivision or specialized subsystem teams.

Shifting from project to product thinking fundamentally changes team incentives. Projects have temporal endpoints; teams complete and move on, leaving operations to manage maintenance and bugs. Products persist; the same cross-functional team owns long-term quality, deployment, and evolution. This ownership model creates accountability—developers take responsibility for quality metrics rather than treating code as someone else's problem once shipped.

Culture and Experimentation: Beyond structure, evolutionary architecture requires a culture that measures and incentivizes evolvability. Architects should assess whether teams understand fitness functions, measure their systems against them, comprehend coupling and connascence, and make tool choices based on changeability rather than technology preference. The adage from Eliyahu Goldratt applies: "Tell me how you measure me, and I will tell you how I will behave." Code reviews become venues for discussing how new code supports future changes, applying the Refactoring principle: do something once, wince at duplication the second time, refactor the third time. This prevents overengineering while maintaining evolvability.

Successful evolution demands experimentation—running small technical and product-level activities, integrating successes into existing systems. Organizations foster this through bringing external ideas (conferences, consultants), encouraging continuous improvement (Toyota's kaizen), implementing spike solutions (throwaway prototypes for learning), allocating innovation time (Google's 20% time, Atlassian's ShipIt days), following set-based development (exploring multiple approaches simultaneously in short prototypes), and connecting engineers directly with end users to understand impact. Facebook's photo-flagging case exemplifies this: facing overwhelming false positives, they experimented with different question phrasings to understand why users flagged photos, discovering behavioral quirks that improved their algorithm.

Budgeting and Cost Architecture: An interesting relationship exists between architectural quanta (independently deployable units) and cost. As quantum count rises, cost per quantum initially decreases due to better separation of concerns and mandatory automation. However, extremely small quanta (e.g., services at field granularity) increase coordination costs, creating a sweet spot. Company strategy determines optimal quantum size: aggressive markets may demand smaller quanta for shorter cycle times, while others pragmatically choose monolithic approaches.

Business Case via Hypothesis-Driven Development: Rather than gathering formal requirements, teams leverage the scientific method. They build hypotheses during ideation ("if we increase image size, we expect 5% sales increase"), design experiments (A/B tests), and validate via data. This incorporates users into the feedback loop via behavior observation and A/B testing rather than surveys. The mobile.de case study illustrates teams building three UI versions and letting users decide, breaking the cycle of incremental decay that plagues mature products.

Fitness Functions as Experimental Media: Four concrete PenultimateWidgets case studies demonstrate practical fitness function deployment:

1. UDP Communications: The team questioned whether their custom monitoring tool reliably captured ETL task completions. They built a fitness function calculating estimated message volume, creating a Mock Service to simulate load, capturing metrics in JSON, and analyzing with Pandas. Result: 40% message loss at scale, validating the need to replace the tool.

2. Security Dependencies: A breach in library dependencies drove lengthy manual reviews that hindered velocity. They automated CI/CD security scanning against a real-time blocklist, raising alerts for affected libraries.

3. Concurrency: Strangler Fig pattern migration of a microservice showed crashes despite measured 300 req/s capacity. Their fitness function queried New Relic for production call rates (1,200 req/s—far exceeding their 120 req/s estimate), revealing the actual auto-scaling factor needed.

4. Fidelity: When replacing monolith functionality via the Strangler Fig pattern, they built a fidelity fitness function running old and new implementations side-by-side with thresholds, ensuring behavior equivalence while discovering undocumented data dependencies.

Enterprise-Wide Fitness Functions: Architects no longer own all decisions but provide guidance and enterprise-wide fitness functions. Each deployment pipeline becomes a venue where security teams can inject tests for framework versions (addressing zero-day exploits like Apache Struts CVE-2017-5638 that affected Equifax). This allows enterprises to automate critical governance—scalability, security, supply chain metadata—across all projects simultaneously.

Bounded Contexts and Fitness Functions: Layered architectures (technical partitioning) and domain-driven design (bounded contexts) initially appear incompatible: layering promotes reuse across contexts, while bounded contexts oppose cross-cutting reuse. Teams reconcile this via fitness functions preventing accidental cross-bounded-context coupling, using connascence principles and locality to maintain isolation within a shared infrastructure.

Where to Start: The chapter outlines four entry strategies. Low-hanging fruit targets already-decoupled parts showing quick wins with metrics (before/after proof). Highest-value-first approaches the most critical system parts, signaling commitment and providing actionable data on applicability. Testing-first adds functional tests around behavior before refactoring, combining increased modularity with coarse-grained tests. Infrastructure addresses dysfunctional operations—outsourcing overhead, development-operations firewalls, or technical debt—as a prerequisite.

PenultimateWidgets Enterprise Case: Architects compiled 62 discrete requirements (security, performance, availability, etc.) but realized manual verification was infeasible and blanket policies caused overengineering (do admin screens truly need five nines?). They converted criteria to fitness functions in a deployment pipeline template, having architects define critical cross-cutting concerns (security) while teams add service-specific ones (availability).

Future and Strategic Considerations: The chapter envisions AI-based fitness functions detecting anomalies (fraud detection as a model) and generative testing using statistical analysis of many test runs to uncover unanticipated edge cases rather than relying on manually specified boundaries.

The authors present reasons to adopt evolutionary architecture: predictability has expired due to dynamic equilibrium; scale demands decoupling (Amazon's shift from monolithic database-backed systems); advanced capabilities (A/B testing, hypothesis-driven development) require evolvability; cycle time has become a business differentiator; isolating characteristics per quantum allows trade-off flexibility; and evolution prevents the brittleness and technical debt spiral that adaptation (preserving old behavior alongside new) inevitably creates.

Conversely, legitimate reasons exist to avoid it: Big Balls of Mud may be costlier to evolve than rewrite; other architectural characteristics (performance, security) may dominate; domain-specific architectures optimize for a single concern; sacrificial architectures (MVPs) expect replacement; and short-lifespan ventures don't require evolvability investment.

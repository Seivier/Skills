## Preface: Invalidating Axioms

The section opens with a formal definition of axiom as "a statement or proposition which is regarded as being established, accepted, or self-evidently true." The authors distinguish mathematics—where axioms are immutable foundations—from software, where axioms are fundamentally unstable. They describe the software development ecosystem as existing in "dynamic equilibrium": balanced at any given point in time but exhibiting dynamic behavior over the long term. A cascade effect occurs where small changes cause other small changes; when repeated hundreds of times, these generate entirely new ecosystems. Containerization and Kubernetes serve as a concrete modern example—Kubernetes didn't exist a decade ago yet now entire software conferences service its users.

The authors assert that architects have an important responsibility to question assumptions and axioms left over from previous eras, noting that many influential architecture books were written in eras that "only barely resemble[s] the current world." This theme recurs throughout: the field must continually re-examine fundamental axioms in light of improved engineering practices, operational ecosystems, and software development processes.

The preface traces an evolution of capabilities: Extreme Programming, Continuous Delivery, the DevOps revolution, microservices, containerization, and cloud-based resources all led to new capabilities and trade-offs. As capabilities changed, architects' perspectives shifted. A key turning point is the evolution of the definition of software architecture itself—from "the stuff that's hard to change later" to microservices architecture, where "change is a first-class design consideration." Each new era requires new practices, tools, measurements, patterns, and related innovations.

The book adopts "An Engineering Approach" as its subtitle, reflecting developers' long wish to transform software development from craft (skilled artisans creating one-off works) to engineering discipline (repeatability, rigor, and effective analysis). While software engineering lags behind other disciplines by many orders of magnitude due to software's youth, architects have made huge improvements, particularly through modern Agile engineering practices.

The authors emphasize trade-off analysis as critically important: developers often become enamored with particular technologies, but architects must soberly assess the good, bad, and ugly of every choice. The preface stresses that "virtually nothing in the real world offers convenient binary choices—everything is a trade-off," motivating a pragmatic perspective focused on analyzing trade-offs rather than passing value judgments about technology. The book aims to equip readers with an analytic eye toward technology choices. The authors acknowledge the field is nuanced with many facets, and the book won't create architects overnight, but it aims to provide a modern overview covering structure and soft skills while rethinking existing axioms in light of the current ecosystem.

## Chapter 1. Introduction

The chapter begins by noting the paradox that software architect is a top job yet lacks a defined career path. The industry has struggled to define the discipline itself; Martin Fowler famously refused to define it, instead citing Ralph Johnson's observation that "Architecture is about the important stuff…whatever that is." The authors illustrate this challenge through a mindmap showing the expanding scope of the architect role, which now encompasses technical, operational, and soft-skill dimensions.

The authors propose a comprehensive definition: architecture consists of the structure (the type of architecture style such as microservices, layered, or microkernel), architecture characteristics (the "-ilities" like scalability and availability that define success criteria orthogonal to functionality), architecture decisions (rules constraining how systems should be constructed, e.g., restricting database access to business and services layers), and design principles (guidelines rather than hard rules, such as preferring asynchronous messaging in microservices). When architects cannot follow an architecture decision due to constraints, they seek a variance—an exception reviewed and approved or denied by an Architecture Review Board (ARB).

The chapter identifies eight core expectations of architects: make architecture decisions (guiding rather than specifying technology choices), continually analyze the architecture (assessing architecture vitality—how viable multi-year-old designs remain given changed business and technology contexts, including addressing structural decay), keep current with latest trends (since architectural decisions are long-lasting), ensure compliance with decisions (verifying teams follow documented decisions), have diverse exposure and experience (technical breadth across multiple technologies and platforms rather than depth in one), possess business domain knowledge (needed to communicate credibly with stakeholders), possess interpersonal skills (leadership and teamwork, as Gerald Weinberg observed: "no matter what they tell you, it's always a people problem"), and understand and navigate politics (since almost every architectural decision will be challenged by product owners, project managers, developers, or business stakeholders).

The intersection with engineering practices distinguishes between process (how teams are formed and managed) and engineering practices (process-agnostic proven techniques like continuous integration). The chapter traces this evolution from Extreme Programming (XP), initiated by Kent Beck in March 1996, which emphasized test-first development and automation, through Continuous Delivery, to the DevOps movement. The Pets.com cautionary tale illustrates why elastic scale (the ability to spin up additional resource instances as needed) became essential; the company's failure to scale infrastructure during success demonstrates how unknown unknowns—things no one anticipated—plague software projects. The chapter introduces the concept of evolutionary architecture and fitness functions—objective integrity assessments of architectural characteristics using metrics, unit tests, monitors, and chaos engineering. An example is measuring page load time as part of continuous integration.

DevOps arose partly from recognizing that operations concerns like elastic scale were better handled by operations teams rather than baked into architectures like ESB-driven SOA or Space-Based Architecture. Microservices architecture simplified design by creating a liaison between architects and operations. The chapter emphasizes that Agile methodologies provide faster feedback loops, better supporting iterative architecture and techniques like the Strangler Pattern and feature toggles for architectural migration. The chapter notes that code and data have a symbiotic relationship and introduces the concept of architectural quantum, which includes external concerns such as databases.

## Chapter 2. Architectural Thinking

Architectural thinking differs fundamentally from development thinking. The chapter presents four essential aspects. First, architecture and design are inseparable, contrary to the traditional unidirectional model where architects hand off designs to developers. The traditional approach fails because architectural decisions don't propagate to development teams and development changes affecting architecture don't reach architects. Success requires breaking barriers and forming bidirectional collaboration, with the architect and developer as a same-virtual-team working in synchronization.

Second, architects must prioritize technical breadth over depth. Using the knowledge pyramid framework, knowledge divides into: stuff you know (daily-use expertise), stuff you know you don't know (heard of but lack depth), and stuff you don't know you don't know (unknown unknowns). Early-career developers should expand the pyramid's top for expertise; technical depth is their focus. Architects transitioning from development must shift perspective—knowing five solutions exist for a problem is more valuable than singular expertise in one. Technical depth (top of pyramid) represents what you know deeply; technical breadth (middle section penetrating downward) represents the scope of knowledge. Architects should sacrifice deep expertise to broaden their portfolio, accepting that some expertise areas atrophy while others remain. Two dysfunctions emerge: architects attempting to maintain expertise across many areas without succeeding, and stale expertise (the mistaken belief that outdated information remains cutting edge, seen in founders-turned-leaders making decisions on ancient criteria).

Third, architectural thinking requires analyzing trade-offs. The principle "Architecture is the stuff you can't Google" and "There are no right or wrong answers in architecture—only trade-offs" form the foundation. Every decision involves benefits and negatives. The auction system example illustrates this: a Bid Producer service sends bid data to Bid Capture, Bid Tracking, and Bid Analytics services via either topics (publish-and-subscribe) or queues (point-to-point) messaging. Topics offer architectural extensibility (single producer connection; new services subscribe without system changes) and service decoupling (producer unaware of data usage). However, topics create data access and security vulnerabilities (anyone can wiretap), enforce homogeneous contracts (all services accept identical data), and lack monitoring for auto-scaling. Queues provide security (only intended consumers receive messages), heterogeneous contracts (each consumer specifies needed data independently), and monitoring with programmatic load balancing, but require modifying the producer and adding new queues when services are added. Technology-specific factors also apply—Advanced Message Queuing Protocol (AMQP) separates exchange from queue, enabling monitoring and load balancing. The decision depends on business drivers, environment, deployment factors, budgets, and other contextual elements. Programmers understand benefits; architects must understand both benefits and trade-offs.

Fourth, architects must understand business drivers and translate them into architecture characteristics (scalability, performance, availability). This demands business domain knowledge and collaborative relationships with key stakeholders.

Finally, architects must balance hands-on coding with architectural responsibilities. The bottleneck trap occurs when architects own critical-path or framework code, becoming bottlenecks while juggling developer and architect roles. The solution: delegate critical infrastructure to the team and focus on business-related code, which gains hands-on experience, distributes ownership, and improves identification with development team struggles. When full-time coding is impossible, architects can remain hands-on through: frequent proof-of-concepts (POCs) to validate decisions and understand implementation details and effort required; tackling technical debt stories and architecture stories (low-priority work that doesn't derail iterations); working on bug fixes to identify code base weakness; creating automation tools and command-line analyzers for repetitive team tasks; writing architectural fitness functions and compliance tools (using ArchUnit for Java); and conducting frequent code reviews to ensure architectural compliance and provide mentoring opportunities.

## Chapter 3. Modularity

The chapter begins by untangling the overused and poorly defined term "modularity," noting Glenford J. Myers's observation that 95% of architectural discourse extols modularity without explaining how to achieve it. Modularity is defined as a logical grouping of related code (classes, functions, or other constructs), supported by various language mechanisms: packages in Java, namespaces in .NET, modules in modern languages, and so forth. This is fundamentally about organizing code logically, not necessarily physically, a distinction important when considering monolithic applications that may house many logically separate modules. The authors stress that modularity is an implicit architecture characteristic—virtually no project explicitly requires "ensure good modular distinction," yet sustainable codebases depend on it. Using a physics analogy, they observe that software systems tend toward entropy; architects must continuously expend energy to preserve structural soundness.

The chapter presents the concept of namespace as critical for separating software assets and avoiding name conflicts, illustrating this with Java's design choice to enforce package-directory correspondence and the subsequent evolution of the JAR mechanism and classpath, which introduced new complexity around name collisions and class loader debugging.

The section on measuring modularity introduces three foundational metrics. Cohesion refers to the extent that parts of a module belong together, measured on a spectrum from best to worst: Functional cohesion (all parts related and essential to function), Sequential cohesion (one module's output feeds another's input), Communicational cohesion (modules operating on shared information), Procedural cohesion (code executing in particular order), Temporal cohesion (timing-based relationships, exemplified by initialization at system startup), Logical cohesion (related logically but not functionally, as in the StringUtils package with unrelated static methods), and Coincidental cohesion (elements unrelated except location in same file). Cohesion is acknowledged as less precise than coupling metrics and depends on architect discretion. The authors illustrate the ambiguity by questioning whether "get customer orders" and "cancel customer orders" belong in Customer Maintenance or in a separate Order Maintenance module, noting this decision depends on module scope, growth expectations, and coupling implications.

The Chidamber and Kemerer LCOM (Lack of Cohesion in Methods) metric measures structural cohesion, exposing incidental coupling within classes. LCOM version 1 is presented as Equation 3-1, where P increases for methods not accessing a particular shared field and Q decreases for methods sharing a field. A more recent version, LCOM96b, is shown in Equation 3-2. The practical definition simplifies to: the sum of sets of methods not shared via sharing fields. Three example classes are illustrated (Figure 3-1): Class X shows low (good) LCOM, Class Y shows high lack of cohesion where field/method pairs could be separate classes, and Class Z shows mixed cohesion. LCOM's limitation is that it detects only structural lack of cohesion, not logical fitness. The authors reference their Second Law of Software Architecture (prefer why over how) to note that metrics cannot distinguish logical coherence.

Coupling metrics are introduced through Yourdon and Constantine's Structured Design (1979), which defines afferent coupling (incoming connections) and efferent coupling (outgoing connections). These metrics, applied to code artifacts (components, classes, functions), form the basis for graph-based analysis tools available across platforms. A sidebar explains the confusing naming: the terms differ only in vowels because Yourdon and Constantine prioritized mathematical symmetry over clarity; mnemonics include "a before e in alphabet" (incoming before outgoing) or "e matches exit."

Robert Martin's derived metrics—Abstractness, Instability, and Distance from Main Sequence—extend coupling analysis. Abstractness (Equation 3-3) is the ratio of abstract artifacts (abstract classes, interfaces) to concrete artifacts. A codebase with 5,000 lines in one main() method has abstractness near 0; excessive abstraction (as in AbstractSingletonProxyFactoryBean) creates difficulty. Instability (Equation 3-4) is the ratio of efferent coupling to total coupling; high instability indicates breakage susceptibility. Distance from Main Sequence (Equation 3-5) combines both metrics to identify ideal relationships between abstractness and instability. Classes near the idealized line exhibit healthy balance. Those in the upper-right corner enter the "zone of uselessness" (too abstract to use); those in the lower-left corner enter the "zone of pain" (too concrete, brittle, hard to maintain). Figures 3-2, 3-3, and 3-4 illustrate these concepts. A sidebar notes that metrics are blunt tools requiring interpretation and cannot distinguish essential complexity from accidental complexity.

The longest section addresses Connascence, Meilir Page-Jones's 1996 refinement of coupling concepts for object-oriented design. Two components are connascent if a change in one requires changes in the other to maintain system correctness. Static connascence covers source-code-level coupling: Connascence of Name (CoN, components agreeing on entity names); Connascence of Type (CoT, agreement on variable/parameter types, supported in statically typed and selectively typed languages like Clojure); Connascence of Meaning/Convention (CoM/CoC, agreement on hardcoded values like int TRUE=1 versus int FALSE=0); Connascence of Position (CoP, agreement on parameter order, exemplified by updateSeat("14D", "Ford, N") where types match but semantics fail); and Connascence of Algorithm (CoA, agreement on computation, as in client-server hash algorithms requiring identical results).

Dynamic connascence covers execution-time coupling: Connascence of Execution (CoE, order-dependent operations like Email property setters); Connascence of Timing (CoT, timing dependencies such as race conditions); Connascence of Values (CoV, values that must change together, illustrated by rectangle corner points and distributed transactions requiring atomicity); and Connascence of Identity (CoI, independent components sharing and updating common data like distributed queues). Architects lack tools to analyze dynamic connascence as effectively as call-graph analysis for static coupling.

Connascence properties guide its application. Strength measures refactoring ease; static is preferable to dynamic. Locality considers module proximity: proximal code tolerates higher connascence; separated code requires weaker forms. Degree measures impact scope. Page-Jones offers three guidelines: minimize overall connascence through encapsulation, minimize connascence crossing boundaries, and maximize connascence within boundaries. Jim Weirich popularized connascence and offered two rules: convert strong forms to weaker forms, and as distance increases, use weaker forms.

The chapter unifies coupling and connascence by observing that static connascence represents degrees of incoming or outgoing coupling. Structured programming's data coupling aligns with connascence's detailed guidance on how coupling manifests. A sidebar addresses the limitations of 1990s connascence for modern distributed architectures, noting it doesn't adequately address synchronous versus asynchronous communication decisions necessary in microservices.

## Chapter 4. Architecture Characteristics Defined

The chapter establishes that architects have a unique responsibility beyond coding and design: defining and discovering architecture characteristics. While teams collaborate on domain/business requirements, architects must uncover factors enabling system success that aren't directly domain-related. The terminology debate centers on precision: 'nonfunctional requirements' undermines importance through negative framing; 'quality attributes' suggests post-deployment assessment. Architecture characteristics better captures their role as design considerations critical to system success.

An architecture characteristic must satisfy three interconnected criteria (illustrated as Figure 4-2's triangle): First, it specifies nondomain design consideration—operational and design criteria distinct from functional requirements. Performance is rarely in requirements yet architecturally critical; preventing technical debt never appears in documents yet drives decisions. Second, it influences structural aspects—architects design special structures only when necessary. Security illustrates this: third-party payment integration requires standard hygiene (encryption, hashing) without special structure, but in-application payment processing requires a dedicated module isolating security concerns structurally. Third, it must be critical or important to application success—architects must choose the fewest characteristics necessary, as each adds design complexity.

The authors subdivide characteristics into implicit versus explicit. Implicit characteristics rarely appear in requirements yet are necessary: availability, reliability, security underpin virtually all applications without explicit specification. Architects must use domain knowledge to uncover these. Explicit characteristics appear in requirements documents or specific instructions.

Operational architecture characteristics cover performance, scalability, elasticity, availability, and reliability. Availability defines required system uptime and rapid failure recovery. Continuity provides disaster recovery capability. Performance encompasses stress testing, peak analysis, function frequency analysis, capacity determination, and response times—often requiring months of acceptance testing. Recoverability specifies business continuity timeline (how quickly after disaster systems must be online), affecting backup strategy and hardware duplication. Reliability/safety assesses fail-safe requirements and mission-critical impact affecting company finances or lives. Robustness handles error and boundary conditions during internet loss, power outage, or hardware failure. Scalability enables system operation as user/request numbers increase.

Structural architecture characteristics concern code quality and structure. Configurability allows end-user configuration through usable interfaces. Extensibility measures ability to plug in new functionality. Installability addresses cross-platform installation ease. Leverageability/reuse enables component reuse across products. Localization supports multiple languages, multibyte characters, and cultural units (measure, currency). Maintainability measures change and enhancement ease. Portability determines multi-platform operation capability. Supportability specifies technical support level and logging/debugging facilities. Upgradeability enables version upgrade ease on servers and clients.

Cross-cutting architecture characteristics form important constraints outside easy categorization. Accessibility ensures users with disabilities (colorblindness, hearing loss) can access systems. Archivability addresses data deletion after periods or archival to secondary databases. Authentication ensures users are who they claim. Authorization controls access by use case, subsystem, webpage, business rule, or field level. Legal addresses legislative constraints (data protection, Sarbanes Oxley, GDPR) and company reservation rights. Privacy hides transactions from internal employees through encrypted communications. Security determines database encryption, network encryption between systems, and remote access authentication. Supportability specifies technical support and logging requirements. Usability/achievability measures user training required for goal achievement.

Terminology ambiguity pervades the field. Interoperability implies published, documented APIs enabling easy integration with other systems; compatibility concerns industry and domain standards adherence. Learnability can mean either ease for users to learn software OR the system's ability to self-learn via machine learning. Availability (operational accessibility) and reliability (function under specified conditions for specified time) overlap but differ: UDP illustrates availability without reliability—packets arrive via IP but may be out-of-order with potential loss requiring receiver retransmission.

The ISO (International Organization for Standards) provides comprehensive definitions: Performance efficiency measures performance relative to resources, including time behavior (response, processing times, throughput), resource utilization (amounts and types), and capacity (maximum limit degree). Compatibility enables information exchange while sharing environments, including coexistence (efficient shared operation) and interoperability (system information exchange). Usability enables effective, efficient, satisfactory system use, including appropriateness recognizability, learnability, user error protection, and accessibility.

Reliability measures function under specified conditions for specified periods, including maturity (normal operation reliability), availability (operational accessibility), fault tolerance (operation despite hardware/software faults), and recoverability (failure recovery with data preservation and desired state restoration). Security protects information enabling appropriate access by type and authorization level, including confidentiality (authorized-only access), integrity (preventing unauthorized modification), nonrepudiation (proving events occurred), accountability (tracing user actions), and authenticity (identity proof).

Maintainability measures developer modification effectiveness, including modularity (discrete components), reusability (asset reuse across systems), analyzability (metrics gathering ease), modifiability (change capability without defects), and testability. Portability enables system transfer across environments, including adaptability (hardware/software adaptation), installability (installation/uninstallation capability), and replaceability (functionality replacement ease).

Functional suitability addresses functional aspects (functional completeness, correctness, appropriateness) but the authors argue these represent motivational requirements, not architecture characteristics, reflecting evolved thinking about the domain/characteristic relationship.

Trade-offs and least worst architecture form the chapter's conclusion. Applications cannot support all characteristics due to design effort requirements and because characteristics interact with each other. Security improvements almost certainly degrade performance through on-the-fly encryption, secret indirection, and other activities degrading performance. A helicopter control metaphor illustrates: pilots struggle learning helicopters because controls for each hand and foot interact—changing one impacts others. Similarly, supporting each architecture characteristic potentially complicates overall design, forcing architects to balance competing concerns rather than maximize every characteristic.

Architects rarely design systems maximizing every characteristic; instead decisions involve trade-offs between competing concerns. The core principle: never pursue the 'best' architecture but rather the 'least worst' architecture. Too many characteristics lead to generic solutions attempting to solve every business problem—these architectures fail because designs become unwieldy. Architects should design for architectural iterability, enabling easier change and reducing pressure to get initial decisions perfectly right, aligning with Agile software development's iteration value.

## Chapter 5. Identifying Architectural Characteristics

Identifying architectural characteristics is presented as a foundational activity requiring architects to understand both the domain problem and stakeholder priorities. The authors introduce a critical anti-pattern called "Generic Architecture"—attempting to design systems supporting all characteristics—which leads to unnecessary complexity before addressing the core problem domain. The Vasa case study illustrates this danger: a Swedish warship designed to be both troop transport and gunship with enlarged specifications ultimately failed due to being over-engineered, capsizing on its maiden voyage.

The section establishes that architects discover characteristics through three primary pathways: domain concerns (which require translation between stakeholder and technical languages), explicit requirements statements, and implicit domain knowledge. A key insight presented is that stakeholders and architects speak different languages: stakeholders discuss mergers/acquisitions, user satisfaction, time to market, and competitive advantage, while architects discuss scalability, interoperability, fault tolerance, learnability, and availability. Table 5-1 provides a translation matrix mapping domain concerns to architectural characteristics—mergers/acquisitions map to interoperability, scalability, adaptability, extensibility; time to market to agility, testability, deployability; user satisfaction to performance, availability, fault tolerance, testability, deployability, agility, security; competitive advantage to agility, testability, deployability, scalability, availability, fault tolerance; and time/budget to simplicity and feasibility.

The authors caution against a common trap: confusing individual characteristics with compound characteristics. Agility does not equal time to market; rather, time to market requires agility plus testability plus deployability. A concrete example illustrates this: when stakeholders cite regulatory requirements for end-of-day fund pricing, an architect focusing only on performance will fail. The system also requires availability (if not available, speed is irrelevant), scalability (as the domain grows, processing must scale), reliability (to prevent crashes during critical operations), recoverability (the ability to restart from interruption), and auditability (ensuring correctness of calculations).

The section on extracting from requirements emphasizes that some characteristics appear explicitly in documents (expected user counts and scale), while others emerge from domain knowledge. The authors introduce Architecture Katas—training exercises created by Ted Neward where architects derive characteristics from domain descriptions. Katas include predefined sections: Description (the domain problem), Users (expected number and types), Requirements (domain-level requirements), and Additional Context (implicit considerations not explicitly stated but critical to design).

The Silicon Sandwiches case study demonstrates practical application. The kata describes a national sandwich shop enabling online ordering with thousands of users, possibly millions eventually. Explicit characteristics identified include: (1) Scalability—decoding "thousands, perhaps millions" of users into the ability to handle large concurrent user loads without degradation; (2) Elasticity—the ability to handle traffic bursts, distinguishing it from steady-state scalability. The example notes that concert ticket booking systems need high elasticity while hotel reservation systems may not. (3) Performance—sandwich shop traffic concentrates around mealtimes, creating peak-load considerations. (4) Customizability—derived from requirements for national and local promotions, custom mapping integration, and franchise-specific behaviors. The authors present the microkernel architecture as one option supporting customization, but note that Template Method design pattern could alternatively implement this within a different architectural style.

Implicit characteristics identified for Silicon Sandwiches include: Availability (ensuring users can access the site), Reliability (maintaining connections without forcing re-authentication), and Security (implicit in every system but may not require structural design if third parties handle payments and general hygiene is followed). The authors stress that each characteristic interacts with others, illustrating the pitfall of over-specification, which complicates design as much as under-specification.

The section emphasizes that there is "no best design in architecture, only a least worst collection of trade-offs." A sidebar on "Design Versus Architecture and Trade-Offs" explains that while customizability might be an architecture characteristic requiring structural support (such as microkernel's plug-in architecture), it could also be implemented through design patterns like Template Method within a different architectural style. The authors stress the importance of architectural trade-off analysis, which must not be made in isolation by architects but requires collaboration with developers, project managers, operations teams, and domain analysts. They warn against the "Ivory Tower Architect" anti-pattern—making decisions without implementation team input.

Finally, the authors advise architects to prioritize characteristics by selecting the top three most important (in any order) rather than attempting complete priority ranking, which typically fails to gain consensus. A useful exercise involves identifying the least important characteristic; if elimination is necessary, it reveals which characteristics are truly critical to success. For Silicon Sandwiches, either customizability could be eliminated (moving it to application design) or performance could be de-prioritized (aiming for acceptable rather than optimal performance) in favor of other characteristics like scalability or availability.

## Chapter 6. Measuring and Governing Architecture Characteristics

The chapter begins by identifying three fundamental problems with how organizations define architecture characteristics: they often lack physics-like precision (e.g., "agility" remains vague), definitions vary widely even within the same organization, and many desired characteristics are composite (agility decomposes into modularity, deployability, testability). The solution is establishing objective, organization-wide definitions that create a ubiquitous language around architecture.

Operational measures address characteristics with direct measurable interpretations. Performance exemplifies this domain: teams may measure average response time, but this masks outliers—if 1% of requests take 10 times longer, they disappear in averages. Thus teams establish maximum response times as well. The authors detail performance budgets (e.g., first-page render in 500 ms), K-weight budgets (maximum bytes for page downloads, constrained by physics and mobile latency), and metrics like first contentful paint and first CPU idle. Advanced organizations use statistical models: rather than arbitrary targets, they measure scale over time, build prediction models, and raise alarms when real-time metrics fall outside predictions. This approach helps distinguish whether anomalies indicate model incorrectness or genuine problems.

Structural measures address internal characteristics like modularity. Cyclomatic complexity (CC), developed by Thomas McCabe Sr. in 1976, provides an objective metric using graph theory applied to code decision points. The formula for a single function is CC = E - N + 2, where E = edges (possible decisions) and N = nodes (lines of code). For functions with multiple connected components (fan-out calls to other methods), the formula is CC = E - N + 2P, where P = connected components. An example function with two conditional branches yields CC = 3. The authors present industry thresholds (CC under 10 acceptable, preferably under 5 for cohesive code) but note this depends on problem domain complexity. They cite the Crap4J tool in Java, which combines CC and code coverage to assess code quality—if CC exceeds 50, coverage cannot rescue the code. The authors reference one extreme case: a single C function with CC over 800 and 4,000+ lines, heavily using GOTO statements. Test-driven development, as a positive side effect, produces smaller, less complex methods on average due to its focus on discrete behavior and good test boundaries.

Process measures intersect software development with architecture. Agility is a composite characteristic decomposable into testability and deployability. Testability is measurable through code coverage tools, though 100% coverage with poor assertions provides false confidence. Deployability is measurable via successful-to-failed deployment ratios, deployment duration, and bugs raised by deployments. The authors emphasize that teams must determine their own measurement sets aligned to organizational goals. Importantly, process characteristics like deployability and testability drive structural architecture decisions: prioritizing these forces architects to emphasize modularity and isolation at the architecture level.

Governance derives from Greek "kubernan" (to steer) and represents the architect's responsibility to influence any aspect of software development relevant to architecture. The book Building Evolutionary Architectures introduces fitness functions, a family of techniques for automating architecture governance. Fitness functions borrow from evolutionary computing, where genetic algorithms undergo mutation guided by an objective fitness function measuring output quality. For instance, in the traveling salesperson problem, fitness functions might evaluate route length, cost, or travel time. In architecture, an architecture fitness function is defined as "any mechanism that provides an objective integrity assessment of some architecture characteristic or combination of architecture characteristics." Fitness functions are not new frameworks but rather a perspective on existing tools (metrics, monitors, unit tests, chaos engineering) applied with governance intent.

For modularity, cyclic dependencies present a significant harm—they prevent component reuse without dragging dependent components along, pushing architecture toward the Big Ball of Mud anti-pattern. IDEs' auto-import features exacerbate this risk through developer habit. A fitness function using JDepend, a metrics tool for Java package structure, can detect cycles and fail the build. Example 6-2 shows a JUnit-based test using JDepend to analyze package directories and assert no cycles exist, integrating into continuous builds to prevent accidental cycle introduction.

Distance from the main sequence, an esoteric architectural metric, can also be verified via fitness functions. Example 6-3 demonstrates using JDepend to establish a threshold (ideal distance of 0.0 with project-dependent tolerance of 0.5) and failing tests if packages fall outside acceptable range. The authors stress collaboration between developers and architects: fitness functions should not be developed in an ivory tower without developer understanding and buy-in.

ArchUnit, a Java testing framework inspired by JUnit, codifies governance rules as unit tests for modularity. Layered architectures illustrate its value: architects design layers for specific reasons, but developers may skip layers for performance or other concerns, eroding architectural intent. ArchUnit allows defining layer relationships and verifying them—for example, enforcing that Controller layers may not be accessed by any layer, Service layers only by Controller, and Persistence layers only by Service. The NetArchTest tool provides equivalent functionality for .NET, allowing C# teams to assert that presentation classes should not directly reference data repositories.

Netflix's Chaos Monkey and Simian Army exemplify fitness functions at production scale. Chaos Monkey simulates production chaos to test resilience. The Simian Army extends this with specialized tools: Conformity Monkey enforces governance rules (e.g., all services respond to all RESTful verbs), Security Monkey checks for known defects and configuration errors, and Janitor Monkey identifies orphan services no longer receiving traffic and removes them from the cloud (reducing costs). These tools emerged when Netflix moved to AWS, relinquishing operational control—chaos engineering became a discipline to proactively detect failures rather than passively experiencing them.

The authors invoke The Checklist Manifesto by Atul Gawande, which documents how professions like aviation and surgery use checklists not because professionals forget but because highly detailed work repeated often allows details to slip. This is the correct perspective on fitness functions: not heavyweight governance but mechanisms for architects to express important principles and automatically verify them. Developers know they should avoid insecure code, but dozens of priorities compete for attention. Tools like Security Monkey and fitness functions generally allow architects to codify important checks into the architecture substrate, providing reminders of architectural principles.

## Chapter 7. Scope of Architecture Characteristics

The chapter begins by noting that the axiomatic assumption of system-level architecture characteristics was reasonable a decade ago when monolithic systems dominated but is now outdated with modern architectures. To properly measure the structural evolvability of architecture styles, the authors needed a technique that accounts for dependent components outside the code base—such as databases—that significantly impact architecture characteristics.

The foundation for understanding architecture quantum is connascence, defined by Meilir Page-Jones (1996) as: "Two components are connascent if a change in one would require the other to be modified in order to maintain the overall correctness of the system." Page-Jones identified two types of connascence: static (discoverable via static code analysis, such as two microservices sharing a common class definition) and dynamic (concerning runtime behavior). Dynamic connascence further divides into synchronous connascence (where the caller waits for the callee) and asynchronous connascence (fire-and-forget semantics in event-driven architectures), with asynchronous calls allowing services to differ in operational characteristics.

The authors then define architecture quantum as containing three essential parts: (1) Independently deployable—including all necessary components to function independently, meaning legacy systems with single databases form one quantum by definition, while microservices create multiple quanta through bounded contexts; (2) High functional cohesion—where contained code is unified in purpose (a Customer component with all customer-related properties and methods exhibits high cohesion, whereas a Utility component with miscellaneous methods does not); and (3) Synchronous connascence—where synchronous calls within or between services mean the services cannot have extreme differences in operational characteristics, as if the caller is much more scalable than the callee, timeouts and reliability issues will occur.

The chapter introduces Domain-Driven Design's bounded context concept, which recognizes that entities work best within localized contexts rather than creating unified shared artifacts across organizations, which cause coupling and complexity. The authors illustrate quantum scoping through the Going, Going, Gone architecture kata concerning an online auction company with requirements for nationwide scale, real-time performance, credit card processing, reputation tracking, live video streaming, and message ordering. They systematically analyze each requirement: scalability and elasticity for supporting thousands of participants; security for credit card handling and fraud prevention; auditability and loggability for reputation tracking; and critically, availability requirements that differ across the architecture. This analysis reveals that different architectural quanta—the bidder feedback stream, the auctioneer system, and the online bidders—each require distinct sets of characteristics rather than uniform system-wide requirements. The bidder feedback quantum needs availability, scalability, and performance; the auctioneer needs availability, reliability, scalability, elasticity, performance, and security; and bidders need reliability, availability, scalability, and elasticity.

## Chapter 8. Component-Based Thinking

The chapter begins by distinguishing components from modules: while a module is a logical grouping of related code, a component is the physical packaging of modules, manifest in language-specific mechanisms like jar files in Java, dll in .NET, and gems in Ruby. Components exist at multiple scopes, forming a hierarchy from simple libraries (which run in the same memory space and communicate via function calls, typically compiled as dependencies) through subsystems and layers, to services (which run in separate address spaces, communicate via networking protocols like TCP/IP or REST, and form standalone deployable units in microservices architectures). The chapter emphasizes that component scope varies based on context: in microservices, simplicity is valued, so a service may contain minimal code requiring no internal component structure.

The architect's primary responsibility is to define, refine, manage, and govern components, working collaboratively with developers, business analysts, subject matter experts, and quality assurance staff. Critically, the architect treats the component as the lowest level of direct concern, with exception to code quality metrics that affect codebases holistically; class and function design falls to developers and tech leads, though architects should involve themselves in discovering and applying design patterns without micromanaging each decision. This balance is essential for empowering the next generation of architects.

Architecture partitioning represents a fundamental choice with trade-offs, taking two primary forms: layered monoliths organize code by technical capabilities (presentation, business rules, services, persistence), while modular monoliths organize around business domains and workflows. Technical partitioning enforces separation of technical concerns—the organizing principle that creates useful decoupling, allowing changes in persistence to potentially affect only specific layers—making it logical for developers to locate categories of code. However, most realistic systems feature workflows cutting across technical layers. The CatalogCheckout workflow example illustrates this problem: in a technically partitioned architecture, CatalogCheckout code appears in all layers as "smeared" across the technical structure, whereas domain partitioning organizes top-level components around workflows and domains, better reflecting the changes most commonly required. The authors note an industry trend toward domain partitioning but emphasize this is fundamentally a First Law of Software Architecture trade-off decision.

Domain partitioning, inspired by Eric Evans' Domain-Driven Design (DDD), identifies independent, decoupled domains or workflows. The microservices architecture style is built on this philosophy. In a modular monolith, while components nest and may include internal layers, top-level partitioning revolves around domains rather than technical capabilities. Domain partitioning advantages include modeling alignment with business functions, enabling application of the Inverse Conway Maneuver to build cross-functional teams around domains, alignment with modular monolith and microservices styles, message flows that match the problem domain, and ease of migrating data and components to distributed architectures. The disadvantage is that customization code appears in multiple places. Technical partitioning advantages include clearly separated customization code and alignment with layered architecture patterns; disadvantages include higher global coupling (changes to Common or Local components affect all others), duplication of domain concepts across layers, and higher data-level coupling making later distributed migration difficult.

The component identification flow operates iteratively: architects identify initial components, assign requirements to them, analyze roles and responsibilities, analyze architecture characteristics, and restructure based on feedback. Initial component identification begins with an architect making basic decisions about partitioning (technical or domain), then mapping domain functionality to created components. Assignment of requirements and user stories tests fit, potentially requiring creation of new components, consolidation of existing ones, or splitting components with too much responsibility. Role and responsibility analysis ensures component granularity matches the roles and behaviors the application must support—discovering correct granularity is one of architecture's greatest challenges. Architecture characteristics analysis affects component division: parts handling different scales of concurrency (hundreds vs. a few users) require different characteristics and thus component subdivision. Restructuring is critical because software design benefits from iteration; unexpected difficulties invariably emerge during projects.

Component granularity—the proper size of components—is difficult to achieve. Too fine-grained design creates excessive communication overhead between components; too coarse-grained design results in high internal coupling, reducing deployability and testability with modularity-related side effects.

Four primary component discovery techniques are presented. The entity trap anti-pattern arises when architects incorrectly identify database relationships as workflows, creating a component-relational mapping of a framework to a database—essentially creating a Manager component for each database entity. This indicates lack of thought about actual application workflows and produces overly coarse-grained components offering no guidance to the development team. Naked Objects and Isis frameworks (and Ruby on Rails scaffolding) represent the legitimate inverse: when a system requires only simple CRUD operations, full-blown architecture is unnecessary. The actor/actions approach, originally defined by the Rational Unified Process, identifies actors who perform activities with the application and the actions they perform, providing a technique for discovering typical users and their interactions. This approach works well with distinct roles and specific action types, across both monolithic and distributed systems, and suits formal processes favoring significant upfront design.

Event storming, originating in domain-driven design (DDD) and popular with microservices, assumes projects use messages and events for inter-component communication, determining which events occur based on requirements and identified roles, building components around event and message handlers. This technique aligns well with distributed architectures using events and messages, helping architects define system messages early. The workflow approach, a more generic alternative for architects not using DDD or messaging, models components around workflows without explicit message-based constraints, identifying key roles, determining their workflows, and building components around identified activities.

The Silicon Sandwiches case study illustrates the trade-off between domain and technical partitioning. A domain-partitioned design creates discrete components for Purchase, Promotion, MakeOrder, ManageInventory, Recipes, Delivery, and Location, with subcomponents handling customization (common and local variations). A technically partitioned alternative isolates Common and Local as top-level components with Purchase and Delivery remaining for workflow handling. Domain partitioning advantages include closer business alignment, enabling the Inverse Conway Maneuver, microservices alignment, matching message flows to domains, and easier migration to distributed architectures; the disadvantage is duplicated customization code. Technical partitioning advantages are clear customization separation and layered architecture alignment; disadvantages include global coupling where changes affect all components, duplicated domain concepts, and data-level coupling complicating future distributed migration.

The Going, Going, Gone (GGG) case study demonstrates actor/actions component discovery and subsequent refinement through architecture characteristics analysis. Initial identified roles are bidder, auctioneer, and system (for internal actions), with actions including view live streams, place bids (bidder), enter bids, mark items sold (auctioneer), and start auction, process payment, track activity (system). Initial components identified are VideoStreamer (streams live auction to users), BidStreamer (streams occurring bids, offering read-only views with VideoStreamer), BidCapture (captures bids from auctioneer and bidders), BidTracker (tracks bids, system of record), AuctionSession (starts/stops auctions, handles payment and resolution), and Payment (third-party processor). However, architecture characteristics analysis reveals that bidders and auctioneers require different scalability, reliability, and availability characteristics; while dropped connections or login failures harm bidders, they are disastrous for the auctioneer. This mandates splitting BidCapture into BidCapture and AuctioneerCapture so each supports differing characteristics. BidTracker unifies the distinct information streams: the single auctioneer stream and multiple bidder streams.

The architecture quantum concept—defining the scope of architecture characteristics—determines a final critical choice: monolithic versus distributed architecture. Monolithic architectures feature a single deployable unit including all functionality in one process, typically connected to a single database (including layered and modular monoliths). Distributed architectures consist of multiple services in their own ecosystem communicating via networking, with potentially finer-grained deployment models and independent release cadences. If a system can function with a single quantum (one set of architecture characteristics), monolithic architecture offers advantages. If components require differing characteristics, distributed architecture becomes necessary to accommodate them. GGG's analysis of diverse characteristics among VideoStreamer, BidStreamer, BidCapture variations, and auctioneer versus bidder concerns led to a distributed architecture choice. The architecture quantum analysis enables early determination of this fundamental design choice.

## Part II. Architecture Styles

The introduction establishes the conceptual hierarchy that organizes Part II. An architecture style is defined as the overarching structure governing the organization of user interface and backend source code—whether within layers of a monolithic deployment or as separately deployed services—and how that code interacts with a datastore. Architecture patterns, by contrast, occupy a lower level of abstraction, functioning as design structures that help solve specific problems within a chosen architecture style, such as achieving high scalability or high performance within sets of operations or between sets of services. The section emphasizes that understanding architecture styles represents a major portion of the learning and professional effort for new architects due to their dual importance and abundance. The core architectural principle stressed is that each architecture style encapsulates a well-known set of trade-offs; architects who understand these trade-offs are better positioned to make effective decisions tailored to particular business problems. This framing positions the study of architecture styles as essential to the discipline, making the distinction between styles and patterns crucial for systematic architectural thinking.

## Chapter 9. Foundations

Architecture styles serve as conceptual shorthand in professional discourse, encoding topology, beneficial and detrimental characteristics, deployment models, and data strategies in a single name. The chapter defines fundamental patterns—recurring organizing principles in software architecture history—and explores several key foundational styles.

Big Ball of Mud is presented as an anti-pattern characterized by haphazard structure, spaghetti-code sprawl, and unregulated growth where information becomes global or duplicated. The authors note this anti-pattern occurs despite few architects intending it; projects inadvertently create it through lack of governance. Real-world example shows a Java web application where architectural coupling was so dense that any class change risked unpredictable rippling side effects across the codebase, making change terrifying.

Unitary Architecture describes the original paradigm where software and hardware were singular entities, gradually separating as needs grew. Few unitary architectures exist outside embedded systems because general software systems grow functionally over time, requiring concern separation to maintain performance and scale characteristics.

Client/Server architecture emerged as a fundamental style partitioning systems into frontend and backend. The chapter traces three evolution stages: (1) Desktop + database server, separating presentation logic on desktops from computationally intensive operations on robust database servers via network protocols; (2) Browser + web server, where browsers connect to web servers (themselves connected to databases), creating thinner clients with wider distribution inside and outside firewalls, still considered two-tier because web and database servers run on single operational class machines; (3) Three-tier architecture, popular in the late 1990s, adding application server tiers with industrial-strength databases, application servers managing business logic, and frontend using generated HTML and JavaScript. Three-tier corresponded with network-level protocols like CORBA and DCOM enabling distributed architecture capabilities.

A significant sidebar discusses long-term implications of architectural assumptions: Java was designed during the three-tier era with serialization built into the language core, assuming all future systems would be three-tier. This design decision persists in modern Java despite that architectural style becoming obsolete, constraining language evolution.

The chapter's major focus addresses monolithic versus distributed architecture classification. Monolithic architectures deploy all code in single units; distributed architectures use multiple deployment units connected via remote access protocols. This classification scheme separates styles by the unique challenges distributed systems share but monolithic systems avoid. The chapter enumerates five monolithic styles (Layered, Pipeline, Microkernel) and five distributed styles (Service-based, Event-driven, Space-based, Service-oriented, Microservices).

The eight fallacies of distributed computing—first coined by L. Peter Deutsch and Sun Microsystems colleagues in 1994—describe beliefs assumed true but false: (1) The Network Is Reliable: Networks remain generally unreliable despite improvements; Service A may be healthy but unreachable due to network problems, necessitating timeouts and circuit breakers. Systems relying more heavily on networks become potentially less reliable. (2) Latency Is Zero: Local component calls measure nanoseconds/microseconds while remote access protocol calls measure milliseconds, so t_remote always exceeds t_local. Most architects ignore this; the critical knowledge is 95th-99th percentile latency ("long tail") rather than averages, as chaining ten 100-millisecond service calls adds 1,000 milliseconds to requests. (3) Bandwidth Is Infinite: Monolithic architectures require little bandwidth internally, but distributed services communicating between units significantly consume bandwidth. Stamp coupling exemplifies this: when a wish list service calls customer profile service needing only a name (200 bytes) but receives 45 attributes totaling 500 kb across 2,000 requests per second, the single interservice call consumes 1 Gb of bandwidth. Resolutions include private REST endpoints, field selectors, GraphQL, consumer-driven contracts (CDCs), and internal messaging. (4) The Network Is Secure: VPN and firewall comfort breed complacency; in reality, every distributed deployment unit endpoint requires security against unknown/bad requests, increasing threat surface area by magnitudes versus monolithic systems, contributing to synchronous distributed architecture performance slowness. (5) The Topology Never Changes: Network topology—routers, hubs, switches, firewalls, appliances—constantly changes. A "minor" network upgrade at 2 a.m. can invalidate latency assumptions, triggering timeouts and circuit breakers despite no service deployments, demonstrating why architects must maintain constant communication with operations and network administrators. (6) There Is Only One Administrator: Large companies have dozens of network administrators. Architects must coordinate with multiple parties regarding latency and topology changes, a complexity requirement monolithic applications avoid due to single deployment unit characteristics. (7) Transport Cost Is Zero: Transport cost (not latency) refers to actual monetary cost of remote calls. Distributed architectures cost significantly more than monolithic ones, requiring additional hardware, servers, gateways, firewalls, subnets, proxies. Architects must analyze server/network topology for capacity, bandwidth, latency, and security zones to avoid surprises. (8) The Network Is Homogeneous: Most companies employ multiple network hardware vendors. Heterogeneous vendors do not seamlessly integrate (e.g., Juniper and Cisco), causing network packets to occasionally be lost and impacting reliability, latency, and bandwidth assumptions—fallacies interconnecting in an endless loop.

Beyond the eight fallacies, distributed architectures face additional challenges: (1) Distributed Logging: Root-cause analysis is difficult because logs spread across dozens to hundreds of locations in different formats, unlike monolithic applications with single logs. Tools like Splunk consolidate sources but only scratch the surface. (2) Distributed Transactions: Monolithic architectures leverage straightforward ACID transactions (atomicity, consistency, isolation, durability) through standard commits and rollbacks. Distributed architectures sacrifice data consistency and integrity for scalability, performance, and availability using eventual consistency—data reaches synchronized state at unspecified future points. Transactional sagas manage distributed transactions via event sourcing for compensation or finite state machines. BASE transactions (Basic Availability, Soft State, Eventual Consistency) are techniques where soft state refers to data transit from source to target and inconsistency between sources; systems eventually become consistent through architecture patterns and messaging based on basic availability. (3) Contract Maintenance and Versioning: Creation, maintenance, and versioning of contracts—agreed behavior and data between client and service—proves particularly difficult in distributed architectures with decoupled services owned by different teams, requiring complex version deprecation communication.

## Chapter 10. Layered Architecture Style

The layered architecture is the most common style primarily because of its alignment with typical organizational structures (UI developers, backend developers, DBAs) via Conway's law, combined with simplicity and low cost. It naturally falls into anti-patterns including architecture by implication and accidental architecture when developers lack architectural direction.

The standard topology consists of four layers: presentation (handling UI and browser communication), business (executing business rules and logic), persistence (data access), and database (actual data storage). However, the number and combination of layers varies; three-layer architectures combine business and persistence, while five or more layers exist in complex applications. Physical deployment variants range from combining presentation+business+persistence into one deployment unit with external database, to separating the presentation layer into its own deployment, to embedding all four layers in a single deployment unit—common in on-premises products.

Each layer provides abstraction: the presentation layer doesn't need to know how to retrieve customer data, only to display it; the business layer doesn't concern itself with data formatting or retrieval mechanism, only with business logic. This separation of concerns makes it easy to build effective role and responsibility models, allowing developers to focus technical expertise on their domain. However, this benefit trades off against reduced overall agility.

Layered architecture is technically partitioned by technical role (presentation, business, persistence) rather than domain-partitioned. This means any business domain like "customer" is spread across all layers in the presentation layer, business layer, rules layer, services layer, and database layer, making domain changes difficult and preventing domain-driven design from working well.

Layers can be marked closed or open. Closed layers require requests to pass through sequentially—a request from presentation must go through business then persistence before database. This enforces layers of isolation, where changes in one layer don't impact others (assuming well-defined contracts), enabling independent layer replacement (e.g., replacing JSF with React.js). Open layers allow requests to bypass them, which could improve simple request performance via the fast-lane reader pattern but couples layers and creates brittleness.

To architecturally enforce restrictions like preventing presentation layer access to shared business objects (date/string utilities, auditing, logging classes), architects can add a new services layer marked as open, allowing the business layer to access it directly or bypass it. Proper documentation of which layers are open or closed is critical; failure to do so results in tightly coupled, brittle, difficult-to-test architectures.

The architecture sinkhole anti-pattern occurs when requests pass through layers as simple pass-through processing without business logic at each layer—presentation passes a customer data request to business, which passes it to rules, which passes it to persistence, which queries the database, then data passes back with no aggregation, calculation, or transformation. The 80-20 rule applies: if only 20% of requests are sinkholes it's acceptable, but if 80% are sinkholes the architecture style may be inappropriate. Making all layers open can reduce sinkholes but increases change management difficulty.

Layered architecture is suitable for small, simple applications and websites, particularly as a starting point with tight budget and time constraints. However, as applications grow, maintainability, agility, testability, and deployability are adversely affected.

Architecture characteristics ratings show cost and simplicity as primary strengths (5 stars). Deployability rates very low (1 star) due to deployment ceremony, high risk, and frequent bundling of unrelated changes—a three-line change requires entire deployment unit redeployment. Testability rates low (2 stars) because developers skip regression suites for bundled changes, though mocking and stubbing components helps. Reliability rates medium (3 stars) due to lack of distributed network issues but monolithic deployment risks. Elasticity and scalability rate very low (1 star) because monolithic deployments lack modularity; although scaling specific functions is possible via multithreading and internal messaging, applications can only scale based on a single system quantum. Performance rates low (2 stars) because the style doesn't support parallelism, closed layering, or avoid sinkhole anti-patterns. Fault tolerance is poor—if one part causes out-of-memory condition the entire application crashes. Availability is impacted by high mean-time-to-recovery (MTTR), with startup times ranging from 2 minutes for small applications to 15+ minutes for large applications.

## Chapter 11. Pipeline Architecture Style

The pipeline architecture is one of the fundamental styles in software architecture, originating from Unix terminal shells like Bash and Zsh. Developers working with functional programming languages will recognize parallels with language constructs, and tools using the MapReduce programming model often follow this basic topology. The architecture can be applied to both low-level implementations and higher-level business applications.

The topology consists of pipes and filters. Pipes form the communication channels between filters with one-way, point-to-point communication (rather than broadcast) for performance reasons. Each pipe accepts input from one source and directs output to another, with architects favoring smaller amounts of data to enable high performance.

Filters are self-contained, independent from other filters, and generally stateless, with each performing only one task. Composite tasks are handled by a sequence of filters rather than a single one. Four types of filters exist: Producer (the starting point, outbound only, sometimes called the source); Transformer (accepts input, optionally performs transformation on data, then forwards it to the outbound pipe—functional programmers recognize this as map); Tester (accepts input, tests one or more criteria, then optionally produces output based on the test—similar to reduce in functional programming); and Consumer (the termination point for pipeline flow, sometimes persisting the final result to a database or displaying results on a user interface screen).

The unidirectional nature and simplicity of pipes and filters encourage compositional reuse. A famous illustration is the Doug McIlroy shell script solution to Donald Knuth's text-processing problem, which solved in a Twitter-post-length script what Knuth solved in more than 10 pages of Pascal.

Pipeline architecture appears in various applications facilitating one-way processing, including Electronic Data Interchange (EDI) tools, ETL tools (extract, transform, and load), and orchestrators like Apache Camel. A concrete example involves processing service telemetry through Apache Kafka.

The pipeline architecture is technically partitioned due to the partitioning of application logic into filter types. Because it is usually implemented as a monolithic deployment, the architectural quantum is always one. Cost, simplicity, and modularity are primary strengths. Being monolithic, pipeline architectures lack the complexities of distributed architecture styles, are simple and easy to understand, and are relatively low cost to build and maintain. Modularity is achieved through separation of concerns between filter types and transformers—any filter can be modified or replaced without impacting other filters.

Deployability and testability rate slightly higher than layered architecture due to modularity achieved through filters, but since it remains a monolith, ceremony, risk, frequency of deployment, and testing completion still impact the architecture. Reliability rates medium (three stars) mostly due to lack of network traffic, bandwidth, and latency found in distributed architectures, but is reduced by monolithic deployment issues.

Elasticity and scalability rate very low (one star) due to monolithic deployments. Although certain functions within a monolith might scale more than others, this requires complex design techniques like multithreading, internal messaging, and parallel processing—techniques the architecture is not well suited for. Applications can only scale to a certain point based on the single architecture quantum.

Pipeline architectures do not support fault tolerance due to monolithic deployments and lack of architectural modularity. A small failure in one part can cause out-of-memory conditions affecting the entire application unit. Availability is impacted by high mean time to recovery (MTTR) experienced by monolithic applications, with startup times ranging from 2 minutes for smaller applications up to 15 minutes or more for large applications.

## Chapter 12. Microkernel Architecture Style

The microkernel architecture divides application logic between a core system and independent plug-in components. The core system represents either the minimal functionality required to run the system (as in Eclipse IDE) or the happy path through the application with little custom processing. It can be implemented as a layered architecture, modular monolith, or split into separately deployed domain services, but typically all share a single database. Removing cyclomatic complexity from the core system into plug-in components improves extensibility, maintainability, and testability. Plug-in components are standalone, independent modules containing specialized processing and custom code meant to enhance the core system without dependencies between plugins. Communication between plug-ins and the core system can be point-to-point (method invocation or function call) or remote (REST, messaging). Point-to-point plug-ins are simpler and can be compile-based (requiring full redeployment when modified) or runtime-based (using frameworks like OSGi, Penrose, Jigsaw for Java; Prism for .NET). Remote plug-in access offers better decoupling, scalability, and asynchronous communication but converts the monolithic architecture into a distributed one, increasing complexity and deployment difficulty. Plug-ins typically do not connect directly to the centrally shared database; instead, the core system passes needed data to each plug-in, ensuring database changes only impact the core system. Individual plug-ins can own their own separate data stores, either external or embedded. The microkernel architecture requires a plug-in registry containing information about each plug-in module, including name, data contract, and remote access protocol details. The registry can be as simple as an internal map structure or as complex as an external discovery tool like Apache ZooKeeper or Consul. Contracts between plug-in components and the core system are usually standard across a domain and include behavior, input data, and output data. Contracts are typically implemented in XML, JSON, or as objects passed between plug-in and core system. Custom contracts from third-party plug-ins are adapted using adapters. The architecture characteristics ratings show microkernel as strong in simplicity and cost (five stars), weak in scalability, fault tolerance, and extensibility (one or two stars), and moderate in testability, deployability, reliability, modularity, extensibility, and performance (three stars). The architecture always has a singular quantum due to monolithic deployment. However, microkernel is unique in being both domain-partitioned and technically-partitioned, particularly for applications requiring different configurations per location or strong customization emphasis.

## Chapter 13. Service-Based Architecture Style

Service-based architecture defines a distributed topology consisting of a separately deployed user interface, independently deployed coarse-grained remote services called domain services, and a shared monolithic database. Services are typically deployed as traditional application artifacts (EAR, WAR, or assembly files) without requiring containerization, though containerization is optional. The architecture supports between 4 and 12 domain services with an average of 7, constrained by the single shared database which limits scalability needs. Services are accessed from the user interface via remote protocols including REST (the typical choice), messaging, RPC, or SOAP, using the service locator pattern embedded within the UI, API gateway, or proxy.

The chapter details extensive topology variants that make service-based architecture highly flexible. User interfaces can range from a single monolithic interface to domain-partitioned UI modules matching each domain service. Databases can remain monolithic or be logically partitioned into separate domain-scoped databases, with the critical requirement that data not be duplicated or accessed across service boundaries. An API layer consisting of a reverse proxy or gateway can be introduced between the user interface and services to consolidate cross-cutting concerns like metrics, security, auditing, and service discovery.

Service design within each domain service typically follows a layered architecture pattern with three layers: an API facade layer, a business logic layer, and a persistence layer. Alternatively, domain services can be designed using a modular monolith approach with sub-domains. A critical component is the API access facade, which orchestrates business requests internally—for example, a catalog checkout request would orchestrate internally the operations of placing an order, generating an order ID, applying payment, and updating inventory, all within a single coarse-grained service.

The granularity difference between service-based and microservices architectures creates fundamental trade-offs. Because domain services are coarse-grained, they use traditional ACID (atomicity, consistency, isolation, durability) database transactions with commits and rollbacks, ensuring data integrity. In contrast, microservices use fine-grained services relying on BASE transactions (basic availability, soft state, eventual consistency) that depend on eventual consistency without ACID guarantees. The authors illustrate the integrity difference through a catalog checkout example: in service-based architecture, a failed credit card charge within a single service can be rolled back completely, whereas in microservices multiple services (OrderPlacement and PaymentService) would experience data inconsistency if the payment failed after the order was created. However, service-based architecture has a testing and deployment trade-off: changes to order placement functionality require testing the entire coarse-grained OrderService including payment processing, whereas microservices isolate change impact to a single OrderPlacement service.

Database partitioning addresses the critical challenge of schema change management. A single shared library containing all entity objects represents the least effective implementation pattern because any table schema change requires changes to every service regardless of whether the service accesses the changed table. The more effective approach uses logical database partitioning manifested through federated shared libraries. For example, a database logically partitioned into common, customer, invoicing, order, and tracking domains would have corresponding shared libraries (common_entities_lib, customer_entities_lib, etc.) used only by services in that domain. Changes to invoicing tables impact only the invoicing service and its shared library. A common_entities_lib shared library used across all services requires restricted change access, ideally locked in version control and administered only by the database team to manage the coordination overhead.

The electronics recycling case study demonstrates practical architecture application. The system flows through customer quoting, physical device receiving, device assessment, accounting/payment, item status tracking, recycling/reselling, and periodic reporting. These seven functional areas map directly to seven domain services: Quoting, Receiving, Assessment, Accounting, ItemStatus, Recycling, and Reporting. Scalability is selectively applied: customer-facing Quoting and ItemStatus services support multiple instances with load balancing, while operational services require single instances. Federated user interfaces segregate Customer Facing operations from internal operations (Receiving and Recycling/Accounting). Two separate physical databases provide security boundaries: external customer-facing data in one zone, internal operations in another, with one-way firewall access allowing internal services to update customer information but preventing reverse access.

Architecture characteristics ratings for service-based architecture show four-star ratings in agility, testability, deployability, fault tolerance, and availability, contrasting with lower ratings in scalability (three stars) and elasticity (two stars). The domain-partitioned design means changes within a domain service scope impact only that service, its UI, and its database portion, enabling faster iteration. Because services are self-contained and share databases rather than requiring interservice communication, one service failure doesn't cascade to others. The coarse-grained nature means more functionality is replicated across service instances than in microservices, making programmatic scalability less efficient in machine resource utilization and cost, though scalability remains possible where needed. Service-based architecture achieves simplicity and cost-effectiveness compared to microservices, event-driven, or space-based architectures, making it pragmatically attractive despite lower scalability ratings. Reliability is enhanced due to coarse-grained services producing less network traffic, fewer distributed transactions, and reduced bandwidth usage.

## Chapter 14. Event-Driven Architecture Style

Event-driven architecture comprises decoupled event processing components that asynchronously receive and process events. Most traditional applications use a request-based model where a request orchestrator (UI, API, or ESB) deterministically directs requests to request processors that retrieve or update database information. In contrast, event-based models react to particular situations and take action based on events.

The broker topology distributes message flow across event processor components via a lightweight message broker (RabbitMQ, ActiveMQ, HornetQ). Key components include: initiating event (the starting event), event broker (federated, domain-based clustered instances), event processor (accepts and processes events), and processing events (advertised results of processing). Event processors are highly decoupled and independent. The topology uses publish-and-subscribe messaging with topics. Each processor advertises its results regardless of listeners, providing architectural extensibility—new processors can be added without infrastructure changes. However, disadvantages include lack of workflow control over the overall business transaction, poor error handling when processors fail without visibility, inability to restart business transactions (recoverability), and data inconsistency issues when failures occur undetected.

The mediator topology addresses broker topology shortcomings through a central event mediator managing and controlling workflow coordination. Instead of chain-like broadcasting, the initiating event goes to an event queue, the mediator knows processing steps and generates processing events to dedicated queues in point-to-point fashion, and processors respond back to the mediator rather than broadcasting. Multiple mediators (usually domain-based) reduce single points of failure. Mediator implementations vary by complexity: Apache Camel, Mule ESB, and Spring Integration handle simple error handling and orchestration with custom message flows in code; Apache ODE and Oracle BPEL Process Manager handle complex conditional processing and dynamic paths using Business Process Execution Language (BPEL); Business Process Management (BPM) engines like jBPM handle long-running transactions involving human intervention. A recommended pattern classifies events as simple, hard, or complex, routing all through a simple mediator that either handles the event or delegates to appropriate complex mediators. Unlike broker topology processing events (things that happened), mediator topology processing events are commands (things that need to happen) and must be processed. Mediator topology provides workflow control, error handling, recoverability, and restart capabilities but introduces processor coupling, lower scalability, lower performance, and challenges modeling dynamic processing.

Asynchronous communication offers significant responsiveness improvements. A synchronous RESTful call adding 3,100 milliseconds user latency (50ms receive + 3,000ms processing + 50ms response) becomes 25 milliseconds with asynchronous messaging (25ms queue receipt), though overall processing still takes 3,025 milliseconds. Responsiveness (acknowledging acceptance) differs from performance (end-to-end speed). Main challenges include error handling complexity when responses aren't guaranteed.

The workflow event pattern addresses asynchronous error handling via a workflow delegate. When event consumers experience errors, they immediately delegate to a workflow processor and process the next message, maintaining responsiveness. The workflow processor analyzes errors (static or machine-learning-based), programmatically repairs data, and resubmits to the queue. Unrecoverable errors go to a dashboard for manual intervention. A consequence is out-of-sequence reprocessing; maintaining message order per context (e.g., brokerage account) requires queueing same-context messages until the original error is resolved.

Data loss can occur at three points: message transmission failure to queue, processor crash after dequeuing, or database persistence failure. Prevention techniques: persisted message queues with guaranteed delivery plus synchronous send prevent transmission loss; client acknowledge mode (keeping messages in queue with client ID attached) prevents loss from processor crashes; ACID database commits plus last participant support (LPS) removing messages from queues after database commit prevent database loss.

Broadcast capabilities enable publishing messages without knowledge of receivers or their actions, essential for eventual consistency and complex event processing patterns. Stock ticker broadcasts exemplify this—the service publishing prices broadcasts without knowing how information will be used.

Request-reply messaging addresses scenarios requiring synchronous communication (order IDs, confirmation numbers) via asynchronous infrastructure. Two queues (request and reply) constitute each channel: message producer asynchronously sends to request queue, does blocking wait on reply queue, consumer processes and sends response to reply queue. Two implementation techniques: correlation ID (message header field matching original message ID to filter replies) and temporary queues (dedicated single-use queues named in reply-to header). Correlation ID is recommended for high volumes due to temporary queue broker overhead.

Choose request-based models for well-structured, data-driven requests (customer profile retrieval) when workflow certainty and control matter. Choose event-based models for flexible, action-based events requiring high responsiveness and scale with complex, dynamic processing. Event-based models support dynamic content response, better scalability and elasticity, agility, adaptability, extensibility, responsiveness, performance, and real-time decision-making but only provide eventual consistency, less processing flow control, less outcome certainty, and difficult testing/debugging.

Hybrid architectures combine event-driven with other styles (microservices, space-based architecture). Event-driven architecture removes bottlenecks, provides back pressure points, and adds responsiveness to other styles. Event-driven architecture rates five stars for performance (asynchronous communication with parallel processing), scalability (competing consumers pattern—programmatically add processors as load increases), and fault tolerance (decoupled processors with eventual consistency). It rates low for simplicity and testability due to nondeterministic dynamic event flows creating complex "event tree diagrams" with hundreds to thousands of scenarios. It rates five stars for evolutionary architecture because new features via existing or new processors require minimal infrastructure changes in broker topology.

## Chapter 15. Space-Based Architecture Style

Space-based architecture solves the scalability bottleneck inherent in traditional tiered architectures where database scaling is the most expensive and difficult constraint. The problem is framed as a triangle topology: scaling web servers is easy and inexpensive, scaling application servers is harder, and scaling the database is the hardest. Under high concurrent load, the database becomes the final limiting factor regardless of how many application and web servers are added. The space-based style gets its name from tuple space: using multiple parallel processors that communicate through shared memory.

The general topology comprises six core components. Processing units contain application logic (including web components and backend business logic), an in-memory data grid, and a replication engine implemented through products like Hazelcast, Apache Ignite, or Oracle Coherence. The virtualized middleware manages infrastructure concerns through four subcomponents: the messaging grid (typically a load balancer like HA Proxy or Nginx) determines which processing unit receives a request and can use simple round-robin or complex next-available algorithms; the data grid is the most crucial component, implemented as a replicated cache where each processing unit contains identical data synchronized across all units—asynchronously, typically completing synchronization in under 100 milliseconds; the processing grid is an optional orchestration component managing requests that span multiple processing unit types; and the deployment manager monitors load and response times, dynamically starting and stopping processing unit instances to achieve variable scalability (elasticity). Data pumps send updated data asynchronously to the database via messaging, supporting eventual consistency. Data pumps use contracts specifying actions (add, delete, update) and can be dedicated by cache type (like CustomerProfile, CustomerWishlist) or by domain (like Customer). Data writers accept messages from data pumps and update the database, either as domain-based writers handling all updates in a domain or as dedicated writers aligned to each processing unit. Data readers retrieve data from the database and deliver it to processing units via reverse data pumps, invoked only when all instances of a processing unit crash, during redeployment, or when retrieving archived data.

Each processing unit knows other instances through a member list containing IP addresses and ports. When a new instance starts, it connects and loads the cache from another instance. When an instance goes down, all others automatically update their member lists. The Hazelcast code example shows: `HazelcastInstance hz = Hazelcast.newHazelcastInstance(); Map<String, CustomerProfile> profileCache = hz.getReplicatedMap("CustomerProfile");` All processing units needing customer profile access contain this code, and updates via cache.put() asynchronously replicate to all instances.

Data collisions occur in active/active replicated caching when simultaneous updates happen during replication latency. The authors provide a probabilistic formula: CollisionRate = N*(UR^2)/S*RL, where N is the number of service instances, UR is update rate in milliseconds (squared), S is cache size in rows, and RL is replication latency. A concrete example shows 20 updates/second, 5 instances, 50,000 cache rows, and 100ms latency yields 72,000 updates per hour with only 14.4 collisions (0.02%). Reducing latency to 1 millisecond drops this to 0.1 collisions per hour (0.0002%). Reducing instances from 5 to 2 yields 5.8 collisions per hour; reducing cache size from 50,000 to 10,000 rows yields 72 collisions per hour. Replication latency depends on network type and physical distance between units and must be measured in production.

Space-based architecture uniquely supports hybrid cloud-on-premises deployments: processing units and virtualized middleware run in cloud environments while physical databases remain on-premises, with data synchronization via asynchronous data pumps and eventual consistency.

Replicated caching, where each processing unit contains synchronized identical in-memory data grids, provides extreme performance and fault tolerance (no single point of failure) but has limitations: in-memory caches exceeding 100 MB can reduce elasticity by constraining how many virtual machine instances can be created, and high cache update rates risk data grid inconsistency. Distributed caching uses a centralized external cache server, offering superior consistency but lower performance (remote access latency) and reduced fault tolerance (single point of failure if the cache server goes down, mitigated only by mirroring with potential consistency risks).

The authors recommend choosing based on: replicated cache for relatively static, small data (\<100 MB) prioritizing performance and fault tolerance, and distributed cache for highly dynamic, large data (\>500 MB) prioritizing consistency. They advise using both models within a single application—for example, a distributed cache for inventory counts (highly dynamic, consistency critical) and a replicated cache for reference data like product codes and descriptions (relatively static, performance important).

Near-cache is a hybrid model combining a centralized "full backing cache" (distributed) with "front caches" (in-memory data grids in each processing unit) using eviction policies like Most Recently Used (MRU), Most Frequently Used (MFU), or Random Replacement. However, front caches are not synchronized between processing units, creating inconsistent performance and responsiveness, so the authors do not recommend near-cache for space-based architecture.

Implementation examples include concert ticketing systems, where concurrent users spike from hundreds to thousands instantly when tickets go on sale, requiring rapid seating availability updates and immediate processing of thousands of concurrent requests. The deployment manager would ideally start processing units before sale begins. Online auction systems similarly require handling unpredictable concurrent bidding volumes with high elasticity, and processing units can be devoted to individual auctions, with asynchronous data pumps sending bid data to other processes (bid history, analytics, auditing) without latency.

Architecture characteristics ratings show space-based architecture excels with five-star ratings in elasticity, scalability, and performance—the driving advantages achieved by removing the database constraint and leveraging in-memory caching. However, it trades this for one-star and two-star ratings in simplicity and testability. Simplicity suffers due to caching complexity and eventual consistency risks requiring careful data-loss prevention. Testing receives one star because simulating extreme scalability and elasticity (hundreds of thousands of concurrent users at peak) is prohibitively expensive; most high-volume testing occurs in production, introducing significant operational risk. Cost is high due to caching product licensing and resource utilization demands. Space-based architecture is both domain partitioned (aligning with highly elastic, scalable domains and with processing units acting as domain services) and technically partitioned (separating transactional caching from database storage via data pumps). Architecture quanta are delineated by associations between user interfaces and processing units, with units communicating synchronously within the same quantum, but the database itself is not part of the quantum equation because it does not communicate synchronously with processing units.

## Chapter 16. Orchestration-Driven Service-Oriented Architecture

This architecture emerged from a specific historical moment in the late 1990s when large enterprises were consolidating and scaling rapidly, while computing resources—particularly operating systems and commercial database servers—were expensive and licensed per machine. This external pressure, combined with an organizational philosophy centered on enterprise-level reuse, drove architects toward distributed systems with the explicit goal of gradually building reusable business behavior that wouldn't require constant rewrites across the organization.

The topology consisted of distinct service layers. Business Services sat at the top, representing domain-level entry points (such as ExecuteTrade or PlaceOrder) that could answer the litmus test "Are we in the business of...?" These services contained no code, just input, output, and schema definitions, typically defined by business users. Enterprise Services formed the building blocks beneath them, containing fine-grained, shared implementations like CreateCustomer and CalculateQuote. Application Services represented one-off, single-implementation services owned by individual teams—for example, a geo-location service needed by only one application. Infrastructure Services supplied operational concerns: monitoring, logging, authentication, and authorization, owned by shared infrastructure teams. At the heart sat the Orchestration Engine, which stitched together the entire system, handling transactional coordination, message transformation, and integration with package and legacy systems.

All message flow, even internal calls, routed through the orchestration engine. The example given shows a CreateQuote business service calling the service bus, which defines a workflow consisting of calls to CreateCustomer and CalculateQuote, each of which may call application services. The architecture relied on one or a few centralized relational databases rather than databases per service, with transactional behavior handled declaratively in the orchestration engine.

The core problem: while reuse sounded logical, the architecture created immense coupling. The authors illustrate this with an insurance company example where Customer is extracted as a canonical, reusable service across multiple divisions. Any change to this Customer service ripples to all dependents, making incremental change extremely risky and necessitating coordinated deployments and holistic testing. Moreover, the Consumer service must accommodate all details every division needs—for example, auto insurance requires driver's license information that disability insurance doesn't need, yet disability teams inherit the extra complexity.

The most damaging revelation was the impracticality of such extreme technical partitioning. Domain concepts like CatalogCheckout were spread so thinly—potentially across dozens of services in several tiers plus database schema changes—that they became difficult to modify incrementally. A simple task like "add a new address line to CatalogCheckout" could require touching many services. If enterprise services weren't defined at the correct transactional granularity, developers would need to create near-identical services just to change transactional behavior, defeating the reuse purpose.

Architecture Characteristics ratings exposed further problems. Modern concerns like deployability and testability scored disastrously, partly because they weren't priorities during that era but also because the architecture poorly supported them. Despite being distributed, the architecture maintained a single quantum because it generally used one or a few shared databases and—critically—the orchestration engine acted as a giant coupling point that no part of the architecture could circumvent. This meant service-oriented architecture captured the disadvantages of both monolithic and distributed approaches: tight coupling like a monolith, with the operational complexity and poor performance of distribution. Performance was particularly poor because each business request split across so much of the architecture. Elasticity and scalability were supported (tool vendors invested heavily in session replication), but cost and simplicity had an inverse relationship architects didn't want.

The architecture became an important milestone in teaching architects the difficulty of distributed transactions in practice and the limits of pursuing extreme technical partitioning as an organizational principle.

## Chapter 17. Microservices Architecture

Microservices evolved not from deliberate architectural design but from architects recognizing recurring patterns as the software ecosystem shifted. The term itself was coined early, contrasting microservices with the then-dominant service-oriented architecture (which could have been called 'gigantic services'). However, microservices is deliberately a label, not a description—many developers misinterpret it as prescribing tiny services, when the true goal is capturing domain boundaries.

The architecture is heavily inspired by domain-driven design, specifically the bounded context concept. A bounded context represents decoupling where domains include entities and behaviors (code, database schemas) that are internally coupled but never coupled to anything outside the context. For example, a domain called CatalogCheckout might include catalog items, customers, and payment concepts. The First Law of Software Architecture's central trade-off applies: reuse creates coupling. When architects prioritize high decoupling, they must favor duplication over reuse. Microservices takes this principle to the extreme.

Topologically, each microservice is much smaller than services in orchestration-driven service-oriented architecture. Each service includes all necessary parts to operate independently, including databases and dependent components. Microservices forms a distributed architecture where each service runs in its own process—originally physical machines, evolved to virtual machines and containers. This extreme decoupling solves common problems in multitenant infrastructure: shared application servers eventually hit resource constraints, and improper isolation between shared applications becomes problematic. Cloud resources and container technology now make separate infrastructure practical.

The negative trade-off of distribution is performance—network calls take much longer than method calls, and security verification at every endpoint adds processing overhead, requiring careful granularity decisions. Experienced architects advise against transactions across service boundaries, making service granularity the key to microservices success.

Granularity is where architects struggle most. The common mistake is making services too small, requiring communication links between services to accomplish useful work. Three guidelines help identify appropriate boundaries: Purpose (each service should exhibit extreme functional cohesion, contributing one significant behavior); Transactions (bounded contexts are business workflows, and entities cooperating in transactions often reveal good service boundaries—designing to avoid transactions produces better designs); and Choreography (if services offer excellent domain isolation yet require extensive communication, bundling them into larger services may avoid overhead). Iteration is the only way to ensure good service design.

Data isolation is another bounded context requirement. Microservices avoids shared schemas and databases used as integration points. Architects must avoid the entity trap—not modeling services as single database entities. They must decide how to handle lack of a single source of truth: either identifying one domain as authoritative and coordinating with it, or using database replication and caching. This isolation creates opportunities—teams can choose the most appropriate tool without affecting other teams.

The API layer sits between system consumers and microservices but is optional. It offers useful indirection for proxying and operational facilities like naming services. However, it should not be used as mediator or orchestration tool if architects want to preserve the bounded context philosophy. This illustrates the difference between technical and domain partitioning: mediators appear in technically partitioned architectures, while microservices is firmly domain partitioned.

Operational reuse addresses how to handle architectural aspects benefiting from coupling (monitoring, logging, circuit breakers) while maintaining decoupling. The sidecar pattern places common operational concerns within each service as a separate component. Multiple sidecars connect to form a service mesh, providing unified control for logging, monitoring, and other cross-cutting operational concerns through a service plane interface. Service discovery is used to build elasticity—requests go through a service discovery tool that monitors request frequency and spins up new service instances for scale.

Frontends present two common patterns: monolithic user interface (a single UI calling through the API layer) or microfrontends. Microfrontends utilize components at the UI level to create synchronous granularity matching backend services. Each service emits its own user interface, which the frontend coordinates with other components. Implementation uses component-based frameworks like React or dedicated frameworks.

Communication requires choosing between synchronous and asynchronous. Microservices typically utilizes protocol-aware heterogeneous interoperability: Protocol-aware (services standardize how they call each other), Heterogeneous (each service may use different technology stacks, supporting polyglot environments), and Interoperability (services calling one another via the network). For asynchronous communication, architects use events and messages. Choreography uses no central coordinator, respecting bounded context philosophy. Orchestration uses a localized mediator service to coordinate calls. The First Law applies—choreography preserves decoupling but complicates error handling, while orchestration creates coupling but focuses coordination.

Transactional coordination presents a core challenge. Building transactions across service boundaries violates the core decoupling principle. The best advice is: don't do transactions—fix granularity instead. The saga pattern handles exceptions where services need distinct boundaries yet require transactional coordination. A mediator coordinates the transaction across multiple service calls, recording success/failure. In error conditions, the mediator sends requests to all successful parts instructing them to undo. This compensating transaction framework typically uses a pending state until overall success is indicated. Using the saga pattern sparingly is advised; if transactions are dominant, mistakes were made.

## Chapter 18. Choosing the Appropriate Architecture Style

The chapter opens by establishing that choosing an architecture style requires balancing multiple competing factors. The authors identify six key drivers of shifting architectural fashion in the industry: observations from past painful experiences, constant changes in the software ecosystem, new technological capabilities and paradigm shifts, acceleration of change rates, domain evolution, technology advancement, and external factors like licensing costs. Architects must stay informed about industry trends while making judicious decisions about when to adopt versus when to make exceptions.

The core decision framework requires architects to understand six fundamental areas: the problem domain and its operational implications, the required architecture characteristics that impact system structure, data architecture and schema design, organizational constraints and business factors, project-specific concerns including development process maturity and QA practices, and domain/architecture isomorphism—the alignment between problem topology and architectural shape. The concept of isomorphism is critical: some domains naturally fit certain architectures (e.g., customizable systems suit microkernel patterns), while others are ill-suited (e.g., systems with high semantic coupling struggle with fully decoupled distributed architectures).

From this analysis, architects must make three critical determinations. First, monolith versus distributed: using quantum concepts, architects assess whether a single set of architecture characteristics suffices or whether different system parts need differing characteristics. Second, data location: monolithic systems typically use one or a few relational databases, while distributed architectures require partitioning decisions affecting data flow and workflows. Third, communication style: the authors recommend synchronous communication by default for simplicity, debugging, and reliability, switching to asynchronous only when necessary for performance or scale—though asynchronous introduces complexity around data synchronization, deadlocks, and race conditions.

The Silicon Sandwiches case study demonstrates two monolithic approaches. The modular monolith uses domain-centric components in a single deployment with one relational database, implementing customization through an Override component with an endpoint pattern where each domain component checks this component for customizations. The microkernel variant separates core domain logic from customizations as plug-ins, using the Backends for Frontends (BFF) pattern as a thin adapter layer that translates generic backend information into format-specific outputs for different frontend devices (iOS, web, etc.), enabling device-specific optimizations and future expansion.

The Going, Going, Gone distributed case study uses microservices because different architecture components require differing operational characteristics (availability and scalability differ between auctioneer and bidder roles). The design employs both synchronous and asynchronous communication, choosing asynchronous where components have conflicting operational constraints—for example, if the Payment service processes one payment per 500ms but auctions end simultaneously, synchronous communication would cause timeouts; message queues buffer different processing rates. Quantum analysis revealed five architecture quanta, enabling clearer identification of service, data, and communication boundaries.

## Part III. Techniques and Soft Skills

Part III introduces techniques and soft skills as essential complements to the technical foundations covered earlier. The authors explicitly state that an effective software architect must understand both the technical aspects of software architecture and the primary techniques and soft skills necessary to guide development teams and communicate architecture to stakeholders. The introduction emphasizes that becoming an effective software architect requires mastery in thinking like an architect, team guidance, and stakeholder communication—three core dimensions beyond technical pattern knowledge.

## Chapter 19. Architecture Decisions

The chapter opens by establishing that architecture decisions involve structure and technology choices that impact architecture characteristics. Effective decision-making requires gathering relevant information, providing justification, documenting the decision, and communicating it to stakeholders.

The authors identify three progressive anti-patterns. The Covering Your Assets anti-pattern occurs when architects avoid decisions out of fear of making wrong choices. The solution involves waiting until the last responsible moment—when sufficient information exists to justify the decision without causing analysis paralysis—and collaborating closely with development teams. A concrete example describes a decision to use read-only replicated cache for product data across services: the primary replica would be owned by the catalog service and replicated to other services. While justified for reducing coupling and avoiding interservice calls, development teams discovered the memory requirements exceeded availability, requiring quick architectural adjustment through ongoing collaboration.

The Groundhog Day anti-pattern occurs when people repeatedly discuss decisions without knowing why they were made. This happens because architects fail to provide complete justification. The authors emphasize that decisions require both technical and business justifications. For instance, breaking a monolithic application into separate services has technical merit (decoupling, reduced resource use, independent maintenance), but needs business justification (faster feature delivery, reduced release costs, improved time to market). Four common business justifications are cost, time to market, user satisfaction, and strategic positioning. Without understanding business value, the decision itself should be reconsidered.

The Email-Driven Architecture anti-pattern occurs when decisions are lost, forgotten, or unknown, preventing implementation. Email is a poor document repository. The authors recommend two communication rules: first, never include the actual decision in email body, as this creates multiple systems of record and loses details during updates; instead, mention only the decision's nature and context with a link to a single authoritative source. Second, only notify people directly impacted by the decision.

The authors introduce Michael Nygard's term architecturally significant to distinguish architecture decisions from mere technical decisions. Architecturally significant decisions affect structure (patterns and styles, like microservice bounded contexts), nonfunctional characteristics ("-ilities" like performance), dependencies (coupling points affecting scalability, modularity, agility, testability, reliability), interfaces (how services are accessed, contracts, versioning, deprecation), and construction techniques (platforms, frameworks, tools, processes affecting architecture).

The chapter's main focus is Architecture Decision Records (ADRs), first evangelized by Michael Nygard and adopted by ThoughtWorks Technology Radar. ADRs are short text files (one to two pages) documenting specific decisions, typically written in AsciiDoc or Markdown. ADR-tools (by Nat Pryce) provides command-line interface for numbering, locations, and superseded logic.

ADRs follow a basic structure with seven sections. Title is numbered sequentially and describes the decision concisely. Status can be Proposed, Accepted, or Superseded. The authors recommend establishing criteria for approval: cost (including Full-Time Equivalency rate calculations), cross-team impact, and security implications. They recommend Request for Comments (RFC) status with deadline dates to avoid analysis paralysis. Context specifies the forces driving the decision and describes alternatives. Decision contains the architecture decision with full justification, using affirmative voice ("we will use...") rather than passive opinion. Crucially, it emphasizes the why rather than the how. The Consequences section documents overall impact and trade-off analysis, forcing architects to evaluate whether impacts outweigh benefits. Compliance section (authors' recommendation) documents how the decision will be measured and governed, using fitness functions. Notes section includes metadata: original author, approval date, approved by, superseded date, last modified date, modified by, last modification.

For storage, the authors recommend wiki or shared directory over Git repositories. They recommend hierarchical structure: application directory (subdivided into common for cross-application decisions and subdirectories for specific applications), integration directory (for communication between applications/systems/services), and enterprise directory (global decisions).

The authors position ADRs as effective architecture documentation, complementing C4 Model and ArchiMate standard. ADRs also serve as standards documentation, explaining context, justification, and implications to help developers understand and follow standards. The chapter concludes with the Going, Going, Gone auction system case study, illustrating dozens of architecture decisions including event-driven microservices, split user interfaces, Real-time Transport Protocol (RTP) for video, single API layer, and publish-and-subscribe messaging.

## Chapter 20. Analyzing Architecture Risk

The risk matrix provides the foundational technique for qualifying risk by combining impact (what happens if the risk occurs?) with likelihood (how probable is it?). Each dimension is rated 1 (low), 2 (medium), or 3 (high), and the products yield numerical scores: 1–2 (low risk, green), 3–4 (medium risk, yellow), 6–9 (high risk, red). An example illustrates this: a primary database has high impact (3) if it fails, but if it runs on highly available clustered servers the likelihood is low (1), yielding a score of 3 (medium risk).

Risk assessments build on the matrix by summarizing overall architecture risk across assessment criteria (e.g., availability, performance, scalability, security) organized by services or domain areas. Assessment reports accumulate risk scores both by criteria and by domain to reveal the highest-risk areas and relative risk between domains. Three visualization techniques are discussed: filtering to show only high-risk items (improving signal-to-noise), using plus (+) and minus (−) signs to indicate risk direction (improving/degrading), and using arrows pointing to target numbers to clarify whether risks are trending better or worse.

Risk storming is a collaborative, structured exercise spanning identification, consensus, and mitigation activities. Identification is individual and noncollaborative: each participant, given an architecture diagram and a single risk dimension (such as performance, availability, or security), independently rates areas using the risk matrix and writes risk numbers on colored Post-it notes (green for low, yellow for medium, red for high). Consensus is highly collaborative: all participants place their Post-it notes on a shared (printed or electronic) architecture diagram and discuss discrepancies. When participants disagree, the group discusses the rationale, occasionally revealing overlooked risks or knowledge gaps—for instance, when a participant unfamiliar with a technology marks it as high risk (9), a best practice is to assign the highest rating to unproven or unknown technologies since the risk matrix cannot quantify them. Mitigation, the final activity, seeks architectural changes to reduce or eliminate identified risks; this often incurs cost, requiring stakeholders to decide whether the mitigation expense justifies the risk reduction.

The chapter also notes that risk storming can be applied beyond architecture—for example, during Agile story grooming, using the same matrix to identify stories at high risk of not completing within an iteration.

A detailed case study of a nurse diagnostics call center system demonstrates the method. The system supports 250 nurses nationwide and hundreds of thousands of concurrent self-service patients, integrates with a third-party diagnostics engine (500 requests/second capacity) and a medical records exchange, and must be HIPAA-compliant. Three separate risk storming sessions identified and mitigated risks: (1) Availability: the central database was rated high risk (6, high impact × medium likelihood); the team mitigated this by splitting it into two: a clustered database for nurse profiles and a single-instance database for case notes. External system availability was addressed by researching SLAs (diagnostics engine: 99.99%, medical records: 99.9%). (2) Elasticity: the diagnostics engine interface was high risk (9) due to the 500 req/sec bottleneck and anticipated spikes from outbreaks and flu season; mitigation involved asynchronous message queues between the API gateway and engine, the Ambulance Pattern (prioritizing nurse requests over self-service), and a new Diagnostics Outbreak Cache Server to absorb outbreak-related queries. (3) Security: the single Diagnostics System API gateway was identified as high risk (6, high impact of unauthorized access to medical records × medium likelihood given all user types route through it); the team mitigated by creating separate API gateways for admin, self-service, and nurse traffic, preventing non-nurse interfaces from ever reaching the medical records exchange.

## Chapter 21. Diagramming and Presenting Architecture

The chapter opens with representational consistency as a unifying concept: when architects drill into details of an architecture (like the Silicon Sandwiches plugin structure), they must first establish the relationship between parts within the larger context to prevent viewer confusion.

For diagramming, architects should progress from low-fidelity artifacts (whiteboards, tablets with overhead projectors) to high-fidelity tools. Low-ritual approaches prevent the Irrational Artifact Attachment anti-pattern, where a person's emotional investment in an artifact grows proportionally with the time spent creating it, making them overly reluctant to revise. Once designs stabilize, architects should use professional tools supporting key features: layers (to hide/show details and incrementally build presentation slides), stencils/templates (for consistent visual components across an organization), and magnets (automatic connection points that provide visual alignment).

Three formal diagramming standards are presented: Unified Modeling Language (UML), which unified three 1980s design philosophies but has largely fallen from use except for class and sequence diagrams; C4, developed by Simon Brown, which uses Context (system with users and dependencies), Container (deployment boundaries), Component (architect's logical view), and Class (standard UML diagrams) to provide progressive detail; and ArchiMate, an open-source standard from The Open Group that prioritizes a lightweight, minimal modeling language over exhaustive coverage.

Diagram guidelines emphasize clarity: titles must label all elements clearly; lines should be thick enough to see and use arrows to indicate directional or two-way information flow, with solid lines conventionally representing synchronous communication and dotted lines representing asynchronous communication; shapes lack pervasive standards—the authors use three-dimensional boxes for deployable artifacts and rectangles for containership; labels must disambiguate; color should distinguish artifacts (e.g., different microservices) rather than follow unnecessary monochrome convention; and keys should explain any ambiguous shapes.

The presenting section identifies a fundamental difference between documents and presentations: time. Presentations allow the speaker to control pacing, whereas document readers control their own pace. Two mechanisms manipulate time: transitions (slide-to-slide movement) and animations (movement within a slide, including build in, build out, and actions such as movement or scaling). The Cookie-Cutter anti-pattern warns against artificially padding content to fill slides when ideas don't warrant a predetermined word count, or conversely, forcing oversized ideas onto single slides. Subtle transitions like dissolve stitch slides together narratively, while distinctly different transitions (door or cube) signal topic changes.

Incremental builds combat the Bullet-Riddled Corpse anti-pattern, where every slide contains the speaker's notes projected for all to see. Audiences read text immediately upon appearance, then sit passively while the presenter repeats it verbally. Instead, architects should build up graphical information gradually using animations (like build out on white boxes) to uncover portions of a diagram sequentially, maintaining narrative suspense.

The chapter distinguishes infodecks from presentations: infodecks are emailed like magazine articles and read at the reader's pace with no transitions or animations, containing all information for standalone understanding; presentations use minimal slide content (purposefully half the story) with transitions and animations, relying on the speaker's verbal channel for the other half. Within presentations, slides should not contain comprehensive content—if they did, the presenter could simply email the deck instead. The invisibility pattern leverages this dual-channel principle by inserting blank black slides to refocus all attention onto the speaker when emphasizing critical points.

## Chapter 22. Making Teams Effective

The chapter opens by establishing that architects bear responsibility for guiding teams through architecture implementation, not merely handing off designs. The first concept is team boundaries: architects create constraints that can be too tight (preventing access to necessary tools and practices, causing frustration), too loose (forcing teams to make architectural decisions), or just right (providing appropriate guidance).

Three architect personality types are detailed. Control freak architects micromanage every decision and restriction (naming conventions, class design, method length, even pseudocode), typically because they struggle with transitioning from developer roles and want to define implementation details. The example of a Reference Manager component illustrates this: the architect's role is to identify the component, define core operations like GetData, SetData, ReloadCache, and NotifyOnUpdate, and specify component interactions—not to design internal implementation using patterns like parallel loaders or specific cache structures.

Armchair architects lack hands-on coding experience, don't understand technology or business domains fully, disconnect from teams after initial diagrams, or spread themselves too thin across projects. They produce architecture diagrams that are too high-level to be useful (the trading system example), leaving teams to perform architectural work themselves.

The Elastic Leadership framework, based on Roy Osherove's work but adapted for software architecture, provides five factors to determine appropriate control levels: Team familiarity (new teams need more control for collaboration; familiar teams become self-organizing), Team size (teams larger than 12 require more control; teams of 4 or fewer require less), Overall experience (junior-heavy teams need mentoring; senior teams need less direction), Project complexity (complex projects require more architect availability), and Project duration (short projects need less control because teams sense urgency; long projects need more control to maintain momentum). These factors are scored on a -20 to +20 scale, with negative values indicating armchair tendencies and positive values indicating control freak tendencies. Two detailed scenarios illustrate application: Scenario 1 accumulates -60 points (new team, small size, all experienced, simple project, 2-month duration), suggesting a facilitator role; Scenario 2 accumulates -20 points (familiar team, large size, mostly junior, complex, 6-month duration), suggesting mentoring without disruption.

Three team warning signs indicate problems: Process loss (Brook's law) occurs when actual productivity falls below group potential as team size increases—evidenced by frequent merge conflicts or inability to create parallel work streams. Pluralistic ignorance happens in large teams when members privately reject a norm but publicly agree because they fear appearing foolish or missing something obvious (illustrated by the Emperor's New Clothes analogy). Diffusion of responsibility is when larger team size increases communication difficulty, creating confusion about responsibilities and dropped tasks (illustrated with the highway motorist metaphor).

The chapter covers leveraging checklists, emphasizing that they work when addressing error-prone or frequently-missed steps without procedural dependencies. Three key checklists are most effective: the developer code completion checklist (including items like running code cleanup, checking for absorbed exceptions, and project-specific standards), the unit and functional testing checklist (capturing unusual and edge-case tests that QA discovers), and the software release checklist (addressing configuration changes, third-party libraries, and database updates). The Hawthorne effect is referenced as a technique for ensuring compliance through perceived observation.

Finally, providing guidance through design principles is illustrated using the layered stack (the collection of third-party libraries). Architects guide developers by requiring answers to: Are there overlaps with existing functionality? What is the justification? This prompts developers to question necessity and seek both technical and business justifications. The chapter provides an example sidebar showing how demanding business justification for Scala adoption—even though the Scala enthusiast could provide technical reasons—transformed a disruptive team member into a productive contributor by making him realize the lack of business value.

## Chapter 23. Negotiation and Leadership Skills

The chapter opens by establishing that negotiation and leadership are among the core expectations of a software architect, since almost every architectural decision will be challenged. The authors provide three distinct negotiation contexts, each with specific techniques.

When negotiating with business stakeholders, the chapter emphasizes understanding the political and economic climate. It introduces several key techniques: (1) leveraging grammar and buzzwords to understand stakeholder concerns—phrases like 'zero downtime' reveal that availability is critical, 'this system must be lightning fast' indicates performance concerns, and 'I needed it yesterday' signals time-to-market urgency; (2) gathering information before negotiation, exemplified by Table 23-1, which translates the vernacular of 'nines of availability' into concrete downtime metrics (five nines equals 5 minutes 35 seconds per year; three nines equals 8 hours 46 minutes per year); (3) using cost and time as a last resort in negotiations; and (4) applying the divide-and-conquer rule—qualifying requirements to specific system areas rather than demanding high availability across entire systems, thereby reducing scope and complexity.

For negotiating with peer architects, the chapter contrasts argumentative approaches with the principle that 'demonstration defeats discussion.' Rather than debating REST versus asynchronous messaging in theory, the better approach is running comparative performance tests in production-like environments. The authors stress avoiding argumentativeness and personal attacks, emphasizing that calm leadership and clear reasoning will prevail over heated debate.

When negotiating with developers, effective architects avoid the Ivory Tower Architect anti-pattern—dictating without regard for developer input. Instead, architects should provide justifications for decisions, framing demands as statements of fact rather than personal commands (e.g., 'This means all calls must come from the business layer' instead of 'You must go through the business layer'). A powerful technique is asking developers to arrive at solutions independently; if they fail, they own the reasoning behind the architect's decision, and if they succeed, the architect may have learned something overlooked.

The section on architects as leaders introduces the 4 C's of Architecture: communication, collaboration, clarity, and conciseness—four factors working together to avoid introducing accidental complexity, which the authors define as 'we have made a problem hard' (in contrast to essential complexity, 'we have a hard problem'). The authors quote Neal Ford: 'Developers are drawn to complexity like moths to a flame—frequently with the same result.'

The subsection on being pragmatic yet visionary defines pragmatism as dealing with constraints sensibly (budget, time, team skill level, trade-offs, technical limitations) and visionary as applying imagination and wisdom to future planning. The example of elasticity illustrates this balance: while a data mesh might be theoretically elegant, a pragmatic architect first isolates bottlenecks and explores caching before proposing distributed domain-based databases.

Leading by example emphasizes that respect comes from demonstration, not rank. The authors cite Gerald Weinberg's principle: 'No matter what the problem is, it's a people problem.' Specific techniques include: using collaborative language ('Have you considered using a cache?' rather than 'What you need to do is use a cache'), employing people's names during conversations, firm handshakes with eye contact for professional relationships, and turning requests into favors by framing tasks as requests for help (appealing to basic human nature). Brown-bag lunch sessions showcase the architect's expertise and establish mentoring credibility. The concept of developer flow—a state of complete cognitive engagement where hours feel like minutes—should be protected by scheduling meetings at margins (early morning, after lunch, end of day) rather than during peak creative hours.

Integrating with the development team requires active meeting management. The chapter distinguishes between meetings imposed upon the architect (being invited) and meetings imposed by the architect (calling meetings). For imposed-upon meetings, effective tactics include asking why the architect is needed and requesting the agenda beforehand to determine if attendance is truly necessary. For meetings the architect calls, the author recommends minimizing them, setting clear agendas, and respecting developer flow. Physical presence—sitting with the team rather than in isolated cubicles—signals integration and availability for questions and mentoring.

## Chapter 24. Developing a Career Path

Architects must recognize that technology changes at an accelerating pace and that throwing away obsolete knowledge (like Clipper expertise) and acquiring new knowledge is a defining characteristic of the profession. The book emphasizes the importance of breadth over depth, as illustrated in the knowledge triangle from Chapter 2. The 20-Minute Rule is proposed as a practical technique to carve out dedicated time for learning: devoting at least 20 minutes daily to exploring unfamiliar buzzwords (the things you don't know you don't know) or diving deeper into specific topics to move knowledge from unknown to known. The authors strongly recommend invoking this rule first thing in the morning after getting coffee but before checking email, as lunch and evening hours are typically consumed by work obligations and family commitments. The ThoughtWorks Technology Radar provides a structured framework for this learning effort. It consists of four quadrants covering the software development landscape: Tools (IDEs, integration tools), Languages and frameworks (computer languages, libraries, open source projects), Techniques (software development processes, engineering practices), and Platforms (databases, cloud vendors, operating systems). The radar also contains four rings, from outer to inner: Hold (technologies to avoid or break habits around), Assess (promising but unproven technologies worthy of exploration), Trial (technologies worth pursuing through spike experiments and low-risk pilots to understand capabilities), and Adopt (technologies the organization feels strongly about or best practices worth implementing). For personal use, architects should adapt these meanings: Hold can include both technologies to avoid and bad habits to break; Assess serves as a staging area for future research; Trial indicates active research and spike experiments; Adopt represents technologies the architect is excited about and best practices for solving problems. The exercise of creating a personal radar forces architects to formalize their thinking about technology and balance competing criteria—the cool factor versus job market demand—treating technology selection like financial portfolio management, which demands diversification. Social media and weak links play an important role in technology discovery. Drawing on Andrew McAfee's Enterprise 2.0 concept, the authors explain three types of social contacts: strong links (family, coworkers, regular contacts), weak links (casual acquaintances, distant relatives), and potential links (people not yet met). Weak links are particularly valuable because they offer perspectives outside one's normal experience and are more likely to lead to new opportunities and technologies worth assessing. Architects should build professional social media networks following technologists whose advice they respect to stay current with rapid technological changes. Finally, the chapter emphasizes practice as the proven way to develop skills. The authors reference architecture katas—exercises modeled on martial arts katas—as a way for architects to practice the craft of designing architecture. A key insight is that architecture has no right or wrong answers, only trade-offs. When the authors studied architecture kata solutions from training classes, they found that capturing the topology (how) was less valuable than documenting the reasoning (why), which contains the critical trade-offs and decision logic. Architects are encouraged to always learn, always practice, and actively do architecture work.
